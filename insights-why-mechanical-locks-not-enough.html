Why Mechanical Locks Are Not Enough

Most homes and offices in Singapore still rely on a technology invented in ancient Egypt. Understanding exactly how mechanical locks fail — and how quickly — is what prompts people to actually do something about it.

1. How a mechanical lock actually works

A pin tumbler lock — the standard cylinder lock found on the vast majority of doors in Singapore — operates on a simple principle that has not fundamentally changed since it was patented in the 1860s.

Inside the lock cylinder is a series of spring-loaded pin stacks. Each stack has two pins: a key pin at the bottom and a driver pin above it. When no key is inserted, the driver pins sit across the shear line — the boundary between the rotating cylinder and the fixed housing — and prevent the cylinder from turning.

Insert the correct key and the precisely cut teeth push each key pin to exactly the right height, aligning all the driver pins with the shear line simultaneously. At that moment, the cylinder is free to rotate and the lock opens.

This is elegant engineering. It is also a system with well-understood vulnerabilities that anyone with basic knowledge and a few inexpensive tools can exploit in under a minute.

2. Lock bumping — the attack most people have not heard of

Lock bumping is a technique for opening pin tumbler locks without the correct key. It requires a bump key — a key cut to the maximum depth at every position — and a basic understanding of the physics involved.

The bump key is inserted into the lock one notch short of full depth, then struck sharply while light rotational pressure is applied to the cylinder. The impact travels through the key pins to the driver pins, momentarily causing all the driver pins to jump above the shear line simultaneously. In that fraction of a second, the rotational pressure turns the cylinder and the lock opens.

The critical point is this: bump keys require no skill to acquire and almost no skill to use. They are sold openly. Video demonstrations are widely available. The technique works on the majority of standard pin tumbler locks regardless of brand or price point. A lock that costs $20 and a lock that costs $200 are equally vulnerable if they share the same pin tumbler mechanism.

KEY POINT

Lock bumping leaves no obvious signs of forced entry. A door that has been bumped open looks the same as a door that was opened with the correct key. This is why it is particularly favoured for residential burglaries — the absence of forced entry can complicate insurance claims and investigations.

3. Other ways mechanical locks are bypassed

Lock bumping is one of several techniques. The others are worth understanding because they expose different weaknesses.

  • Credit card or shimming. Spring latch locks can be slipped open with a flexible card or shim slid into the gap between the door and the frame. Many residential doors in Singapore use spring latches as the primary or only lock.
  • Door frame attack. Strike plates with short screws into softwood frames are a common weak point in Singapore terrace house doors. A single kick can dislodge the deadbolt by fracturing the frame.
  • Key duplication. Keys left with domestic helpers, contractors, or neighbours create copies that may persist long after the person's access was intended to end. There is no way to know how many copies exist.
  • Worn locks. Older locks develop loose tolerances, making them significantly easier to pick or bump than a new one.

4. What digital locks actually solve

A digital door lock replaces the mechanical cylinder with an electronic credential — PIN, card, fingerprint, or face — and an electric lock bolt. The vulnerabilities of the pin tumbler mechanism are removed entirely because there is no pin tumbler.

PIN access eliminates the physical key. Access is revoked by changing the PIN, which takes thirty seconds. Fingerprint or face recognition eliminates the credential entirely. Card access allows lost cards to be deactivated instantly.

Every digital lock records every entry. You can see who opened the door and when. This is information that a mechanical lock cannot provide under any circumstances.

5. The Singapore context — why this matters more here

Singapore's high-density residential environment creates specific vulnerabilities. HDB corridors provide cover for burglars to work on front doors. In landed housing, side gates often have cheap, neglected padlocks.

Key management in multi-generational households is often informal. When staff or helpers leave, the lock is rarely changed. For commercial tenants, re-keying is disruptive and expensive, so it is often deferred indefinitely, leaving the business vulnerable to former employees.

The mechanical lock is a nineteenth century solution to a twenty-first century threat environment. It is not that it does not work — it is that it works until the moment someone who knows what they are doing decides to open it. A digital conversion is a one-time cost that eliminates an entire category of vulnerability permanently.
