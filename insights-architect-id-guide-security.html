The Architect and ID's Guide to Security Systems in Singapore — Speccing, Code, Coordination and Tender Documentation

Security systems are the owner's decision. Your job is to incorporate that decision into the drawings, coordinate it across disciplines, and make sure it passes code. This guide covers everything you need to do that well — from schematic design to tender documentation.

Ler Wee Meng Founder · NUS Engineering · UOL Law · 37 years experience
  • Home
  • Insights
  • For Trade Professionals
  • Architect and ID Guide

Security systems sit in an uncomfortable position in most building projects. They are not quite M&E. They are not quite IT. They involve civil works, electrical infrastructure, network cabling, and software platforms — often specified by four different contractors who are not naturally inclined to coordinate with each other.

The owner decides what systems they want. The integrator designs and installs them. But it is the architect or ID who has to incorporate those decisions into the drawings, ensure the rough-ins are called out correctly, coordinate between M&E and IT during construction, and produce documentation that satisfies both the client and the regulatory authorities.

This guide is written for that role. It does not tell you which systems to choose — that is the owner's decision and the integrator's recommendation. It tells you what you need to know to incorporate security systems into your project competently, coordinate them without conflict, and document them for tender and submission purposes.

The five topics covered are the five questions we hear most consistently from architects, IDs, and consultants across Singapore — regardless of project type, project stage, or building use.

Question One

1. How Do I Spec a Security System Into New Build Drawings?

The most important thing to understand about speccing security systems into drawings is that security is a layered discipline — and each layer has different spatial, electrical, and network requirements that need to be captured at different stages of your drawing set.

The four layers of a typical security system are surveillance, access control, vehicle management, and platform integration. Each one needs to appear in your drawings in a specific way.

Surveillance — CCTV and cameras:

Camera positions are the starting point. Every camera needs a defined mounting position — wall-mounted, ceiling-mounted, or pole-mounted for external cameras — and a field of view that covers the intended zone without blind spots. For new builds, camera positions should be coordinated with the architect's elevation drawings to ensure mounting surfaces exist at the right height and orientation.

Each camera position needs a data point and a power point called out in the electrical drawings. IP cameras are powered over ethernet — PoE — which means a single CAT6 cable carries both data and power from the nearest network switch. The switch location, switch room, and cable routing path all need to be identified in the M&E drawings.

For external cameras, conduit routing from the camera position to the nearest junction box or IDF room needs to be shown in the civil works drawings. External conduit should be specified as heavy-duty UV-resistant PVC or galvanised steel for exposed runs.

NVR or server room location needs to be identified early. The recording infrastructure for a medium to large project — NVR, network switches, UPS — requires a dedicated rack in a secure, air-conditioned room. This room needs power, network, and physical security, and its location affects cable run lengths and therefore camera count per switch.

Access control:

Every electronically controlled door needs four things called out in the drawings: the door controller location, the reader position, the locking device type, and the power supply arrangement.

Door controllers — the hardware that processes access decisions — are typically located in an IDF room or dedicated security panel room. Cable runs from each controlled door to the controller need to be shown in the M&E drawings, with conduit sizes specified based on cable count.

Reader positions need to be coordinated with the ID drawings for wall finish and mounting height. Standard mounting height for access readers in Singapore is 900mm to 1050mm from finished floor level — consistent with universal design guidelines.

Locking devices — EM locks, electric strikes, drop bolts — need to be specified in the door schedule with their fail-safe or fail-secure designation clearly noted. This designation affects the fire alarm integration requirement, which is covered in detail in the code requirements section below.

Power supplies for locking devices need to be called out in the electrical drawings. EM locks and electric strikes require a 12V or 24V DC power supply located near the controlled door — typically within the door frame cavity or an adjacent wall panel.

Early coordination between the architect and security engineer prevents costly site variations.

Vehicle management — gantries and ANPR:

Gantry positions need to be incorporated into the site plan early — they affect traffic flow, queuing space, guard house positioning, and drainage. A gantry that is positioned too close to the site entrance creates queuing on the public road. One that is positioned too far creates an uncontrolled zone between the entrance and the gantry.

Each gantry position needs civil works — a concrete base pad to the manufacturer's specification, conduit routing for power and data, and drainage for the gantry housing. These civil works need to be in the builder's scope, not the security integrator's scope, or they will be missed.

ANPR cameras for gantry systems need a specific mounting position — typically on a dedicated pole or overhead structure at the correct angle and distance from the gantry to read vehicle plates reliably. This position and its structural support need to be in the drawings before the builder prices the civil works.

Platform and network infrastructure:

The network infrastructure that carries security data — cameras, access control, intercoms — needs to be designed as part of the building's overall IT infrastructure, not as an afterthought. Security systems increasingly run on the same physical network as building management systems and IT, which means VLAN configuration, network switch specifications, and server room design need to be coordinated between the IT consultant, the M&E engineer, and the security integrator before tender.

KEY POINT

When to engage the security integrator in your drawing process: The earlier the better — ideally at schematic design stage, before M&E coordination drawings are issued. An integrator engaged at schematic stage can provide camera coverage zones, access control door schedules, gantry position requirements, and network infrastructure needs that can be incorporated into the coordinated M&E drawings from the start.

Question Two

2. What Are the Singapore-Specific Code Requirements for Security Systems?

Three regulatory frameworks directly affect how security systems must be installed in Singapore buildings. All three have implications for your drawings and your submission documentation.

SCDF Fire Code — the most commonly missed requirement:

The Singapore Civil Defence Force Fire Code requires that any electronic locking device installed on a door in a means of egress must fail safely in a fire emergency. In plain language: when the fire alarm activates, all electronically locked doors on evacuation routes must unlock automatically — without manual intervention, without power to the locking device, and without requiring anyone to press a button.

This is called fire alarm integration, and it is a mandatory requirement for all electronic locking devices on means of egress. It is not optional. It is not something that can be value-engineered out.

What this means for your drawings: every EM lock, electric strike, or electronic drop bolt on a fire exit or evacuation corridor must be shown with a connection to the building's fire alarm panel. The fire alarm integration wiring needs to appear in the M&E drawings and be confirmed by the FSSD-approved fire protection engineer.

The locking device's fail-safe designation — fail-safe meaning it unlocks when power is lost, fail-secure meaning it locks when power is lost — must match its position relative to evacuation routes. Fail-safe devices on evacuation routes. Fail-secure devices only in locations where locking during a fire is acceptable — which is rare and requires specific fire engineer justification.

Clear documentation of fire alarm integration is critical for SCDF submission approval.

BCA regulations and electrical safety standards:

All security system installations must comply with BCA regulations and the Singapore Standard for Electrical Wiring — SS638. For security systems, the relevant requirements are cable containment, grounding, surge protection, and the involvement of a Licensed Electrical Worker for work above 32A.

For large security installations — typically commercial, institutional, or large residential estates — a Licensed Electrical Worker needs to be involved in the electrical works associated with the security system. This includes the main power supply to the security panel room, the distribution board circuit for security system power supplies, and any high-voltage work associated with outdoor camera or gantry installations.

SPF Private Security Industry Act — the licensing requirement:

Under the Private Security Industry Act, only contractors licensed by the Singapore Police Force are permitted to install, maintain, or repair security systems in Singapore. This is a statutory requirement that affects your tender documentation — only SPF-licensed contractors should be included on your tender list for security systems.

When preparing tender documentation, require every security systems tenderer to provide their SPF licence number as a mandatory submission item. Any tenderer who cannot provide a current, verifiable SPF licence should be disqualified from the tender.

Question Three

3. What Are the Standard Rough-In Requirements Per System Type?

Rough-in requirements are the civil, electrical, and data provisions that the builder must complete before the security integrator can install equipment. Getting these right — and clearly specced in your drawings — is the difference between a clean installation and a variation-heavy one.

CCTV and IP cameras:

  • Data conduit: 25mm diameter minimum PVC conduit from camera mounting position to nearest IDF room or junction box.
  • Cable: CAT6 UTP or CAT6A FTP from camera to PoE switch. Maximum run length 90 metres for passive PoE.
  • Mounting provision: Flush-mounted back box at camera position for ceiling-mounted cameras. Wall bracket mounting plate with M6 anchor bolts for external wall-mounted cameras.
  • Power: PoE cameras do not require a separate power point at the camera position — power is carried over the CAT6 cable.

Access control — door controllers and readers:

  • Conduit: 20mm diameter PVC conduit from reader mounting position to door controller location.
  • Door frame provision: For EM locks and electric strikes, a cable chase within the door frame is required to route the lock power cable. This needs to be coordinated with the door supplier.
  • Power supply: A 240V AC power point is required within 500mm of the door controller or power supply location.
  • UPS provision: Access control power supplies should be on a UPS circuit to maintain function during a power outage.

Verification of rough-in placement during early construction stages prevents expensive remedial works later.

Intercoms and video door phones:

For apartment intercoms serving individual units, a CAT6 or dedicated intercom cable from the main entrance panel to each unit needs to be called out in the electrical drawings. In multi-storey residential buildings, this typically runs through the telephone riser or a dedicated security riser.

At the main entrance panel position, a 240V AC power point and a data conduit to the IDF room are required. For video intercoms, the indoor station requires a 240V AC power point within the unit — typically adjacent to the main entrance of the unit.

Gantry systems:

  • Civil works: Concrete base pad to manufacturer's specification — typically 600mm x 600mm x 300mm deep, with conduit sleeves cast in.
  • Power: A 240V AC single-phase power supply to the gantry housing.
  • Data: CAT6 conduit from gantry position to guard house or IDF room.
  • Drainage: Gantry housings need drainage provision to prevent water ingress. A floor drain or surface channel adjacent to the gantry base pad should be specified.
Question Four

4. How Do I Coordinate Between M&E, Security, and IT Contractors?

Security system coordination failures are one of the most consistent sources of construction variations in Singapore building projects. The reasons are structural — security sits between M&E, IT, and civil works, and none of those disciplines considers security their primary responsibility.

Establish accountability at the start of the project:

The first coordination question is: who is responsible for security system drawings in the M&E coordination set? If there is no specialist consultant, the electrical engineer needs to be explicitly briefed to incorporate security rough-ins into the M&E coordination drawings from the start of design development.

A dedicated coordination meeting between trades is the most effective way to eliminate interface gaps.

The four coordination interfaces that cause the most problems:

  1. Security and Electrical: Power supplies for security equipment need to appear on the electrical engineer's single-line diagram. Require the security integrator to submit a power schedule at design development stage.
  2. Security and IT: Security systems share physical network infrastructure. The integrator and IT consultant need to agree on VLAN segmentation and switch port allocation before the network is specified.
  3. Security and Civil Works: Gantry pads and external conduits are civil works items often missed. Include a security provisional sum in the civil works contract.
  4. Security and Door Supplier: EM locks mount within the door frame. Cable routing requires a chase within the door leaf or frame — coordinate this with the door supplier before fabrication.
Question Five

5. Can I Get Drawings and Specs for Tender Documentation?

Yes — and this is one of the most practical ways a security integrator can support your project, regardless of whether they are the eventual appointed contractor.

Here is what professional tender documentation for a security system should contain:

  • Scope of works description: A plain-language description of what the security system covers.
  • Performance specification: Functional requirements for each system type — resolution, retention period, integration requirements.
  • Schedule of equipment: A line-item list of every component with quantity and specification.
  • Schematic drawings: Camera coverage zones, door schedules, and network topology diagrams.
  • Bill of quantities: Priced BQ items that the QS can incorporate into the main contract.
  • Compliance requirements: SPF licence requirement, BCA compliance statement, and SCDF fire integration confirmation.

Standardised tender documentation ensures comparable quotes and protects against unforeseen costs.

Engage Early. Coordinate Deliberately. Document Everything.

The three principles that produce well-integrated security systems on Singapore building projects are consistent across every project type — residential, commercial, or institutional.

  1. Engage the security integrator at schematic design stage, not tender stage.
  2. Coordinate deliberately between M&E, IT, civil works, and the door supplier.
  3. Document the owner's system decisions clearly in the project records to protect against scope change disputes.

If you are working on a project where the security scope is still being defined, or where you need pre-tender support — schematic drawings, performance specifications, or a coordination review of your M&E drawings — we are set up to provide that support. It is a practical service that costs you nothing and saves everyone on the project significant variation management later.
