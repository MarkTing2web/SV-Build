How to Maintain Your Burglar Alarm

An alarm system that has never been tested is an alarm system you cannot rely on. Components fail, batteries age, and environments change. Regular maintenance is what ensures your system works when it is actually needed.

1. The Monthly Walk Test

You should walk-test your system every month. This does not involve triggering the siren. Most keypads have a "Walk Test" mode that beeps when a sensor detects you but doesn't trigger the alarm.

Walk through every room and open every protected door. Check your app or keypad to ensure each zone registers correctly. If a sensor fails to pick you up until you are halfway across the room, it may have a dirty lens or a failing component.

2. Battery Life — the 3-year rule

The backup battery in your main control panel is the most critical component. It keeps the system alive during power outages. In Singapore's humidity, these batteries typically last between 3 to 4 years.

If your keypad shows a "Low Battery" or "Comm Fail" message, replace the battery immediately. A failing panel battery can cause the entire system to behave erratically, triggering random false alarms at night as the voltage fluctuates.

3. Cleaning your detectors

Motion sensors (PIRs) are optics-based. Dust, cobwebs, and even small insects on the lens can significantly reduce their range or cause false alarms. Every six months, gently wipe the exterior of your sensors with a dry, lint-free cloth. Do not use cleaning chemicals, as these can damage the Fresnel lens on the sensor front.

CHECKLIST

Check your external siren (bell box) once a year. The battery inside the siren is separate from the panel battery and is often forgotten. If it fails, the siren may not sound if a burglar cuts the wires to the main panel.

Maintenance isn't about complexity; it's about consistency. Spend 5 minutes a month on a walk test and 15 minutes once a year checking batteries. That small investment in time ensures that your security system isn't just an expensive ornament on the wall.
