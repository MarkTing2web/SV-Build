What Does a Maintenance Contract Include?

Understanding exactly what you are paying for — and what should be non-negotiable — in any security maintenance agreement in Singapore.

Ler Wee Meng Founder & CEO · Securevision · 7 min read
  • Home
  • Insights
  • What Does a Maintenance Contract Include?

Why the Contract Wording Matters

A maintenance contract for a security system is not the same as a warranty. It is a service commitment with defined scope, response times, and exclusions. The distinction matters because most disputes between building owners and security contractors arise not from bad faith, but from misread scope — a client who expected on-site response within four hours against a contract that commits only to next-business-day attendance.

In Singapore, where commercial tenants, MCST committees, and facilities managers often inherit contracts signed years earlier by different teams, it is worth spending thirty minutes reading what the agreement actually says before a fault occurs.

KEY POINTS

The three things every maintenance contract must define clearly: what is covered, how fast the contractor must respond, and what happens when a part needs replacing.

Scheduled Preventive Maintenance — What It Should Cover

Preventive maintenance (PM) is the proactive element of the contract — work done on a schedule regardless of whether anything has failed. For a security system, a well-structured PM visit should include:

  • CCTV Systems: Camera lens cleaning, housing inspection, IR illuminator check, cable and connector inspection, NVR/DVR health check including storage status and recording verification, remote access and app connectivity test, firmware version check.
  • Access Control: Reader cleaning and test, credential database backup, door hardware inspection (locks, closers, hinges), controller diagnostic, battery backup test on door power supplies, audit log review.
  • Burglar Alarm: Motion detector lens cleaning and sensitivity test, door and window contact inspection, siren and strobe test, control panel battery test, communication path test (SIM/broadband).
  • Intercom/Video Door Phone: Camera and speaker cleaning, call functionality test, door release relay test, power supply check.

A contract that promises a single annual PM visit for a multi-system site is insufficient. For most commercial premises, a minimum of two PM visits per year is the engineering standard.

Corrective Maintenance and Response SLAs

Response times for critical faults are a cornerstone of any professional maintenance agreement.

Corrective maintenance is the reactive element — what happens when something breaks. The contract must specify:

  • Response time. This is the time from fault notification to a technician being on-site. Common tiers in Singapore are: critical (four-hour response, typically for 24-hour operations such as logistics hubs or childcare centres), standard (next business day), and non-critical (within forty-eight to seventy-two hours).
  • Resolution time. Distinct from response time. A technician may arrive within four hours but require a part that takes three days to source. The contract should specify what interim measures are taken — a loan unit, a temporary bypass — while awaiting resolution.
  • Escalation path. Who do you call if the technician cannot resolve the fault? What is the escalation to engineering management? For critical sites, this chain must be named in the contract.

Parts and Labour: What Is Typically Excluded

Most maintenance contracts cover labour but exclude parts — meaning the cost of a replacement camera, controller, or power supply falls to the client. This is standard and reasonable for hardware failures caused by age or external damage.

However, the exclusions section of many contracts goes further and warrants scrutiny:

  • Storm or power surge damage. Typically excluded under an act-of-God clause. Singapore's lightning exposure makes this a material exclusion — surge protection on NVRs and panels is a reasonable self-protection measure.
  • Vandalism or accidental damage. Almost always excluded. Verify whether your property insurance covers this.
  • Software or firmware issues. Some contracts explicitly exclude "software-related faults." In a networked IP system, this exclusion is significant — firmware bugs, VMS crashes, and remote access failures are among the most common service calls.
  • Consumables. Batteries, fuses, and cleaning materials are routinely excluded. These are low-cost but should be accounted for in your annual maintenance budget.

Remote Monitoring and Health Checks

A growing number of maintenance contracts now include a remote health-check component — an automated or manually reviewed check of device status, storage health, and connectivity, conducted without a site visit.

For Singapore commercial sites, this typically means a monthly or quarterly report showing: number of cameras online vs. offline, NVR storage utilisation, failed recording alerts, and access control communication status.

KEY POINTS

Remote health checks do not replace on-site PM visits. They are a supplementary layer that catches faults between scheduled visits — particularly recording failures that would otherwise go undetected until an incident requires footage retrieval.

Documentation and Reporting

A professional maintenance contract should produce documentation after every visit. At minimum, this means a service report signed by both the technician and the site representative, listing every device inspected, every fault found, and every remedial action taken or recommended.

For ISO-certified organisations, MOM-regulated workplaces, or MCST-managed developments, this documentation forms part of the facility management audit trail. Verbal reports or unsigned job sheets are not acceptable for these contexts.

What a Good Contract Looks Like

Methodical documentation ensures an audit trail for compliance and safety standards.

A well-structured maintenance agreement for a Singapore security system will include, at minimum: a defined scope of systems covered listed by type and quantity, PM visit frequency and what each visit includes, corrective maintenance response SLAs tiered by severity, a clear statement of parts policy (covered or client-chargeable), a remote monitoring or health-check provision, a documentation and reporting commitment, and a contract review clause — typically annual — to allow scope adjustment as the site evolves.

What it will not include is vague language such as "regular maintenance as required" or "response as soon as practicable." These phrases are unenforceable and are the source of most disputes.

Securevision Verdict

A maintenance contract is only as good as its specificity. Before signing, ask three questions: What exactly does each PM visit cover? What is the on-site response time for a critical fault? And who bears the cost of replacement parts? If the contractor cannot answer all three clearly, the contract is not ready to sign. Securevision's maintenance agreements are built around defined scope, named SLAs, and full post-visit documentation — because clarity protects both parties.
