Definitions & Core Functions

1. What is an Intercom System?

An intercom system is a standalone communication network designed for voice and video conversation within a building or property.

Unlike telephone systems that connect to external phone lines, intercoms operate as closed networks dedicated to internal communication and visitor management. The core function of an intercom is simple: enable two people in different locations within the same property to communicate instantly. A visitor at your front gate can speak with someone inside the house. An employee in one office can call a colleague in another department. A person trapped in a lift can reach security personnel for help.

The Evolution of Intercom Technology

Intercom systems have transformed dramatically over the past six decades:

1960s-1980s: Audio Handsets

Early intercoms were simple audio-only devices with handsets similar to telephones. A visitor pressed a button at the door station, a bell rang inside, and residents picked up a handset to speak. These systems used basic analog circuitry and required dedicated wiring between the door and indoor stations. Door release was manual—press a button on the handset to trigger a relay that unlocked the gate or door.

1990s: Video Integration

The introduction of small CRT (cathode ray tube) monitors brought visual verification to intercoms. Residents could now see who was at the door before opening. Video quality was modest—grainy black-and-white or low-resolution color—but the security improvement was significant. These systems required more complex wiring: separate cables for audio, video, and power.

2000s: Digital IP Systems

Network-based intercoms emerged, using the same infrastructure as computers and security cameras. IP (Internet Protocol) intercoms transmitted digital audio and video over CAT6 Ethernet cables, enabling higher quality, longer distances, and integration with other building systems. Recording became standard, and systems could support hundreds or thousands of stations on a single network.

2010s-Present: Cloud and Mobile Apps

Modern intercoms connect to the internet, allowing residents to answer door calls on their smartphones from anywhere in the world. Cloud-based platforms manage user permissions, log all visitor interactions, and integrate with access control systems. Facial recognition, QR codes, and temporary digital keys have replaced traditional methods of visitor management.

What Intercoms Are NOT

Understanding what an intercom is not helps clarify its purpose:

Not a Telephone System

Intercoms do not connect to the public switched telephone network (PSTN) or make external calls. You cannot dial a phone number on an intercom. If a system allows calling outside phone numbers, it's a business telephone system (PABX) with door phone capability, not a pure intercom. We cover this distinction in detail in Section 3.

Not a PA (Public Address) System

PA systems broadcast announcements one-way to multiple locations—a voice from the office goes to all zones, but listeners cannot reply. Intercoms are primarily two-way: both parties can speak and hear each other in real-time conversation. Some advanced intercoms include PA capability as an additional feature, but it's not their primary function.

Not a Video Doorbell

Standalone video doorbells like Ring or Nest connect wirelessly to your home WiFi and send notifications to your phone. They're designed as retrofit products for homes without existing intercom infrastructure. Intercoms are complete wired systems with dedicated indoor monitors, multiple stations, and professional-grade reliability. Video doorbells cost $150-$400 and run on batteries or doorbell transformers. Intercom systems cost $900-$5,000+ and integrate with property access control, multiple indoor monitors, and building management systems.

Not a Walkie-Talkie

Walkie-talkies are portable handheld radios for mobile communication across distances (typically 1-5km depending on terrain and power). Intercoms are fixed installations with stations mounted at specific locations. Walkie-talkies use radio frequencies and require users to press-and-hold a talk button (half-duplex). Modern intercoms support full-duplex communication where both parties can speak simultaneously, just like a phone call.

Modern Intercom Capabilities

Today's intercom systems extend far beyond simple voice communication:

High-Definition Video

Modern video intercoms feature 2-megapixel to 4-megapixel cameras with wide-angle lenses (typically 140-180 degrees) and HD or Full HD resolution. Night vision with infrared LEDs allows clear identification of visitors even in complete darkness. Some models include dual cameras—one for facial recognition at eye level, another for package detection at waist level.

Mobile App Integration

Residents receive push notifications when someone presses the door button, see live video on their smartphones, speak with visitors through the app, and release the door remotely—whether they're at home, in the office, or traveling abroad. Call logs and recorded video provide a complete history of all visitor interactions.

Access Control Integration

Many modern door stations include built-in card readers (RFID, NFC), numeric keypads, fingerprint sensors, or facial recognition cameras. Authorized users can enter without calling anyone—they simply tap their card, enter their PIN, or look at the camera. This unifies visitor management (unknown guests call for entry) with resident access (authorized users enter directly) in a single device.

Call Recording and Logging

IP-based intercoms automatically capture snapshots or video recordings of everyone who calls, time-stamped and stored in cloud or local servers. This creates an audit trail for security purposes and helps residents identify previous visitors. Some systems also record the audio conversation, though this requires compliance with Singapore's Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) regarding notification and consent.

Weather Resistance and Durability

Outdoor intercom stations are built to IP65 or IP66 weatherproof ratings, withstanding Singapore's tropical climate: heavy rain, high humidity, intense sun, and temperature variations. Metal housings (stainless steel or aluminum) resist corrosion, and conformal coating protects circuit boards from moisture.

Where Intercoms Are Used

[Six-panel grid showing intercom applications: residential gate station, condominium lobby panel, office reception intercom, warehouse loading bay station, hospital nurse call station, elevator emergency intercom]

Residential Properties

Landed homes, bungalows, and terraced houses install intercoms at main gates and side entrances. Indoor monitors are placed in kitchens, living rooms, or bedrooms, allowing residents to vet visitors without walking to the gate. Systems typically support 1 door station and 2-8 indoor monitors.

Condominiums and Apartments

Multi-unit residential buildings use visitor call panels at main entrances where visitors select a unit number to call. The system routes the call to that unit's indoor monitor or mobile app. Residents see and speak with the visitor, then grant access by pressing a button that releases the lobby door. Large condominiums may have 50 to 5,000 individual units, multiple entrance stations, and guard house integration.

Offices and Commercial Buildings

Businesses install intercoms at reception areas to vet visitors before granting access. Some offices use intercom networks for internal communication between departments, meeting rooms, or floors—similar to an internal telephone extension system but dedicated to building communication rather than external calling.

Industrial Facilities

Warehouses, factories, and manufacturing plants deploy intercoms at loading bays, perimeter gates, and between production areas. Noisy industrial environments require intercoms with high-power speakers, noise-canceling microphones, and rugged construction to withstand harsh conditions.

Institutional Settings

Hospitals, schools, prisons, airports, and railway stations use specialized intercom systems for operational communication and emergency response. These may include hundreds of stations networked together, allowing selective calling between specific locations and centralized monitoring at control rooms.

Emergency Communication

Building codes require intercoms in certain locations for safety: inside elevator cabins (to call for help if trapped), handicap-accessible toilets (emergency call buttons), car park help points (for drivers needing assistance), and fire stairwells (for firefighter coordination). These are often one-way call systems where the person presses a button to alert security or building management.

Intercom systems have evolved from simple voice boxes to sophisticated communication and security platforms. They serve a single unifying purpose: enable people within a property to communicate effectively, verify visitors before granting access, and maintain secure control over who enters the premises. The technology may vary—from basic audio handsets to cloud-connected video systems with artificial intelligence—but the fundamental goal remains constant: safe, convenient communication within your property's boundaries.

Technology Comparisons

2. Intercom vs Other Systems

Intercom systems are often confused with other communication technologies. While there's some overlap in functionality, each system serves distinct purposes. Understanding these differences helps you choose the right solution for your needs.

Intercom vs Walkie-Talkies

Walkie-Talkies: Mobile Communication

Walkie-talkies are portable handheld radios designed for mobile communication across distances. Security guards patrolling a facility, event staff coordinating crowd management, or construction workers moving across a site carry walkie-talkies to stay in contact while on the move.

Key characteristics:

  • Portable: Handheld devices clipping to belt/pocket
  • Radio Frequency: Transmit via VHF/UHF, no cables
  • Range: 1-5km urban, up to 30km open terrain
  • Half-Duplex: Press-to-talk (one person at a time)
  • Group: Everyone on same channel hears all
  • No Installation: Works out-of-the-box
  • Battery Powered: Requires regular charging

Intercoms: Fixed Installation

Intercoms are permanently installed at specific locations within a building or property. A visitor stands at a fixed door station at your gate, presses a button, and the call rings at fixed indoor monitors inside your home.

Key characteristics:

  • Fixed Location: Mounted at specific doors/rooms/lifts
  • Wired Connection: Typically 2-wire, 4-wire, or CAT6
  • Unlimited Range: Works across any building size
  • Full-Duplex: Simultaneous two-way conversation
  • Point-to-Point: Calls directed to specific stations
  • Professional Installation: Requires wiring/mounting
  • Continuous Power: Powered by AC/PoE (no batteries)

When You Need Both:

Many facilities use intercoms AND walkie-talkies for different purposes. A condominium might have:
• Intercoms for visitor communication (gate stations calling fixed unit monitors)
• Walkie-talkies for security guards on patrol (mobile communication while walking grounds)

Each serves its purpose: intercoms for location-based communication, walkie-talkies for people-based communication.

Intercom vs PA (Public Address) Systems

PA Systems: One-Way Broadcasting

PA systems broadcast announcements from a single source to multiple speakers across a facility. A shopping mall plays background music and makes promotional announcements. A factory announces shift changes across the plant.

  • One-Way: Audio flows to speakers only, no reply possible
  • Broadcasting: Same message to all selected zones
  • Mass Communication: Designed to reach many simultaneously
  • Amplifier-Based: Central amplifier drives passive speakers

Intercoms: Two-Way Conversation

Intercoms enable dialogue between specific locations. A visitor calls, you answer, you have a conversation, and decide whether to grant access. Both parties can speak and hear.

  • Two-Way: Both parties speak and listen in real-time
  • Targeted: Call goes to specific station(s) only
  • Interactive: Questions/answers can be exchanged instantly
  • Individual Addressing: Call specific rooms/departments

When They Overlap: Some advanced intercom systems include PA capability as a secondary feature. A building manager might use the intercom infrastructure to broadcast fire drill announcements. However, the primary design remains targeted, interactive dialogue.

Intercom vs Video Doorbells

Video Doorbells: Standalone WiFi Devices

Video doorbells like Ring, Nest Hello, or Arlo replace your existing doorbell button. They connect to WiFi and send video to your smartphone. While excellent for retrofits, they cost $150-$400 and depend entirely on internet and phone connectivity.

Intercoms: Complete Wired Systems

Intercoms are professional systems with dedicated indoor monitors (7-10 inch screens) and wired infrastructure (2-wire/CAT6). They integrate with building access control, work offline if needed, and cost $900-$5,000+ for a complete residential system.

3. Intercom vs IP Phone Systems (Door Phone Capability)

This is where confusion often arises. Both intercom systems and IP phone systems can handle door phones. Both can show video of visitors. Both can release doors. Yet they serve fundamentally different primary purposes.

The Fundamental Difference

Intercom System: Internal Communication Only

An intercom is a closed network for communication within your building. It cannot call external phone numbers or connect to SIP trunks for outside calling. When a visitor calls, it rings wall-mounted monitors or residents' mobile phone apps. It's a one-way communication intake: visitors calling in, or internal stations calling each other.

IP Phone System: Business Telephony

An IP phone system (PABX) is designed for business telephony—making and receiving calls with the outside world. These systems connect to SIP trunks to reach the public network, allowing you to dial external numbers, transfer calls between extensions, set up voicemail, and manage professional call queues.

Where They Overlap: Door Phone Integration

Modern door phones use SIP protocol—the standard that IP phones use. This allows for two distinct architectural approaches:

Approach 1: Intercom System (Traditional)

The door station calls dedicated video intercom monitors. Residents see visitors on a 7-10 inch wall-mounted screen and press a dedicated release button. This is the classic setup found in landed homes and condominiums.

Approach 2: IP Phone System (Converged)

The SIP door station is registered as an extension of the office IP phone system. It calls the receptionist's desk phone, who sees the video feed on the phone screen and unlocks the door using a keypad code (e.g., *9).

[Two side-by-side diagrams: LEFT showing door station calling dedicated intercom monitor, RIGHT showing door station calling desk IP phone]

When to Use a Standalone Intercom System

Residential Homes & Condominiums

Landed properties and apartments don't need business phone features. Residents simply want to see and speak with visitors at the gate. A dedicated wall-mounted monitor in the kitchen serves this purpose perfectly and is far more intuitive for non-technical users.

Small Offices Without PABX

Offices where staff use mobile phones for business don't need the complexity of a phone system just for the door. A simple video intercom at reception provides professional visitor vetting at a lower cost.

Emergency Communication

Intercoms in elevators or handicap toilets must be simple, dedicated safety devices. They cannot depend on a desk phone system that might be managed by IT or turned off after hours.

Budget-Conscious Applications

If your only requirement is visitor communication, installing a dedicated intercom typically costs 40-60% less than deploying a full IP phone system with door integration.

When to Use IP Phone System with Door Phone

  • Offices with Existing PABX: Leverage existing PoE switches and phone servers for incremental cost savings ($600-$900 for just the door phone).
  • Multi-Department Organizations: Use powerful call routing to present menus: "Press 1 for Sales, 2 for Support," routing calls to appropriate desk extensions.
  • Heavy Call Volume: Receptionists can manage customer calls and door calls on one unified interface without switching devices.
  • Unified Communications: Integrate door call logs into the same ecosystem as business calls, presence status, and mobility apps.
  • Future Scalability: Ideal for organizations planning to scale from 5 to 50+ staff across multiple branch offices.

The Hybrid Approach: Best of Both Worlds

Large office buildings often deploy both: a standalone intercom for continuous security monitoring at the guard desk, and IP phone registration for staff convenience when the guard is away.

Technical Convergence: SIP Protocol

SIP (Session Initiation Protocol) is the reason these systems can talk to each other. Modern door phones, monitors, and desk phones all speak the same language, allowing a single station to ring a wall monitor and a desk phone simultaneously or sequentially.

Technical Architecture

4. How Intercom Systems Work

Understanding the fundamental components and signal flow of an intercom system helps you make informed decisions about installation, troubleshooting, and system expansion. While modern intercoms can be sophisticated, the core principles remain straightforward.

Core Components

Every intercom system, regardless of complexity, consists of four essential elements:

1. Door Station (Outdoor Unit)

The door station is the visitor-facing component mounted outside your property—at the gate, front door, or building entrance. It contains:

  • Microphone: Captures the visitor's voice. Outdoor intercoms use noise-canceling microphones to reduce wind, rain, and traffic noise. Microphone sensitivity is calibrated for typical speaking distance (30-60cm from the unit).
  • Speaker: Outputs your voice so the visitor can hear your response. Outdoor speakers must be loud enough (typically 85-95 dB) to be heard in noisy environments and weather-resistant to prevent water damage.
  • Camera (Video Intercoms): Captures visual image of the visitor. Modern door stations use 2-4 megapixel cameras with wide-angle lenses (140-180 degrees) to show not just the visitor's face but also if they're alone and what they're carrying. Infrared LEDs (typically 6-12 LEDs) provide night vision in complete darkness up to 1.5-3 meters.
  • Call Button: The visitor presses this to initiate the call. Simple systems have a single button. Multi-unit systems have a numeric keypad or touchscreen directory where visitors select which unit to call.
  • Door Release Relay: An electronic switch that triggers when you press the unlock button, sending power to your electric lock, magnetic lock, or gate motor to grant access. Relays are rated for the voltage and current of your lock (typically 12V DC or 24V DC, 1-3 amps).
  • Weatherproof Housing: Door stations are built to IP65 or IP66 ratings, meaning they're completely sealed against dust and protected against water jets from any direction. Materials include stainless steel, aluminum, or ruggedized polycarbonate plastic. Singapore's tropical climate demands corrosion resistance and UV stability.
  • Card Reader / Keypad (Optional): Many modern door stations integrate access control, allowing authorized users to enter without calling anyone. RFID card readers support proximity cards, numeric keypads accept PIN codes, and advanced models include fingerprint sensors or facial recognition cameras.
[Exploded diagram of video door station showing microphone, speaker, camera, IR LEDs, call button, relay, and weatherproof housing components labeled]

2. Indoor Station (Monitor or Handset)

The indoor station is where you receive and respond to calls from the door station. This can be:

Handset Intercom (Audio-Only)

Similar to a telephone handset, you pick it up when the door rings, speak into it, and hang up when done. Handset intercoms are simple, familiar to elderly users, and provide audio privacy (conversation isn't broadcast to the room). They're typically audio-only, though some models have small screens showing which door is calling.

Video Monitor (Hands-Free)

A wall-mounted screen (typically 7-10 inches) that displays video from the door camera. Modern monitors are touchscreen, allowing you to answer with a button press, adjust volume, view recorded visitors, and configure settings. Hands-free operation means you don't pick up anything—speak toward the monitor and it transmits your voice via built-in microphone and speaker.

Indoor stations include:

  • Screen: LCD/OLED, 7-10 inch, up to 1080p
  • Speaker: Internal, typically 1-2 watts
  • Microphone: Built-in for hands-free
  • Answer/Release: Touchscreen or physical
  • Volume: Ringtone and talk levels
  • Power: Low-voltage (12-24V) or PoE

3. Controller (Brain of the System)

The controller manages call routing, signal processing, and power distribution. In simple systems, the controller might be built into the door station or indoor monitor. In complex systems, it's a separate unit.

  • Call Routing: Manages logic for which monitors ring when a door button is pressed.
  • Signal Processing: Amplifies signals, filters noise, and mixes audio sources during calls.
  • Power Distribution: Supplies regulated current to stations and locks via the signal cabling.
  • Integration Interface: Connects with access databases, CCTV recording, and BMS logging.

4. Wiring (Communication Medium)

Wiring carries signals and power between components. The type of wiring depends on the intercom technology:

2-Wire Systems

A single pair of wires carries audio and power. Simple and economical for basic homes. Typical range: 100-300m.

4-Wire Systems

Separate pairs for audio and video signals. Used in traditional analog video intercoms often with coaxial cable.

CAT6 Ethernet Cable

IP-based standard. Carries HD video, audio, and power (PoE) in a single digital cable. Max 100m per run.

Wireless (WiFi/4G)

Eliminates cable runs but introduces dependency on signal quality and external internet connectivity.

Signal Flow: What Happens When Someone Presses Your Doorbell

Let's trace a complete interaction from button press to door opening:

1
Visitor Presses Button

The visitor presses the call button, closing an electrical contact that signals the system a call is being initiated.

2
Signal Transmits to Indoor Station

A "call request" (voltage change or SIP INVITE packet) is sent to the indoor monitor(s). The controller determines which monitors ring.

3
Indoor Station Rings

The monitor wakes up, plays a ringtone, and displays live video. You see the visitor and decide whether to answer.

4
You Answer the Call

Answering establishes a two-way audio connection. Echo cancellation filters out speaker-to-microphone feedback for a clear conversation.

5
Conversation

You speak with the visitor to verify their purpose. This vetting stage is the core security function of the intercom.

6
Door Release (Optional)

Pressing the release button triggers the door station's relay, unlocking the electric/magnetic lock or opening the gate for a preset duration.

7
Call Ends

The connection terminates, returning the system to standby. Recordings are saved with timestamps if the system has logging capability.

Communication Types: How Audio is Transmitted

Full-Duplex

Both parties speak and hear simultaneously, like a phone call. Uses echo cancellation to prevent feedback. standard in modern IP systems.

Half-Duplex

Only one person can speak at a time. Requires waiting for silence before replying. Common in budget analog models.

Press-to-Talk (PTT)

Manual audio activation. Hold button to speak, release to listen. Used in very noisy industrial and emergency environments.

[Three-panel diagram showing full-duplex (two-way arrows with simultaneous speech), half-duplex (single alternating arrows), and press-to-talk (button icon with directed arrow)]

Power Distribution in Intercom Systems

How power is delivered affects installation complexity and reliability:

Centralized Power

A single transformer powers all stations. Easier to maintain and back up with batteries, but susceptible to voltage drop over long cable runs.

Distributed Power

Small adapters at each station. Simple to install without cross-building wiring, but requires outlets everywhere and is hard to back up.

Power over Ethernet (PoE)

Data and power on one CAT6 cable. Centralized at the network switch, professional, and very easy to protect via UPS.

[Three diagrams showing centralized power architecture, distributed power with multiple adapters, and PoE network with switch]

Analog vs Digital Signal Processing

Analog Signals

Continuous electrical waveforms represent audio/video. Simple and robust but degrades over distance, susceptible to interference, and capped at standard definition quality.

Digital Signals

Data converted into 1s and 0s using SIP protocol. Maintains perfect quality regardless of distance (within network limits), allows for encryption, and supports HD/4K resolutions.

[Split diagram showing analog waveform degrading over long cable distance versus digital packets maintaining integrity]

System Architectures: How Stations Connect

Point-to-Point

One door station wired directly to one monitor. Maximum simplicity for basic homes.

Star Topology

All stations wire back to a central controller hub. Used in analog multi-station systems.

Daisy Chain

Stations linked in series. Reduces cabling but failure in one station can affect the chain.

Network Topology

IP devices on standard network switches. Maximum scalability across buildings or cities.

[Four network topology diagrams showing point-to-point, star, daisy chain, and networked architectures]

Inside the Hardware

Door Station Event Loop

  • Detection: Microcontroller senses button press.
  • Activation: Firmware wakes camera and IR LEDs.
  • Signaling: Tones (Analog) or SIP INVITE (Digital) sent.
  • Audio Ready: Codecs and amplifiers initialized.
  • Feedback: Status LED indicates active dialing.

Indoor Station Response

  • Wakeup: Screen powers to full brightness.
  • Alert: Configuration-based ringtone plays.
  • Visual: Live video feed decodes onto display.
  • Interactivity: UI displays call control prompts.
  • Log: Snapshot image captured and timestamped.

The intercom system is fundamentally a specialized communication appliance. While modern features like facial recognition and cloud integration add sophistication, the core principle remains constant: connect people across distances within a property for secure communication and reliable access control.

Categorization & Selection

5. Types of Intercom Systems

Intercom systems can be categorized in several ways based on their communication capabilities, underlying technology, installation method, scale, and intended application. Understanding these categories helps you identify which type best suits your property and requirements.

By Communication Capability: Audio vs Video

Audio-Only Intercoms

Audio intercoms transmit voice only—no camera, no visual component. The door station has a microphone, speaker, and call button. The indoor station is a handset (like a telephone) or a simple speaker box with buttons.

  • • Door Station Cost: $150-$400
  • • Indoor Handset Cost: $100-$300
  • • Complete System (1 door + 2 handsets): $400-$1,200
When audio-only makes sense:
  • Budget-conscious applications prioritized over visual verification.
  • Retrofits where only 2-wire cabling exists and running new cables is disruptive.
  • Gated communities where visitors are pre-screened at a main guardhouse.
  • Harsh environments where camera lenses require frequent cleaning.
  • Privacy-conscious users who prefer not to have video recording.
  • Elderly users comfortable with traditional telephone-style handsets.

Audio-Video Intercoms

Audio-video intercoms add a camera to the door station and a screen to the indoor station. You see and speak with visitors simultaneously.

  • • Door Station Cost: $400-$800
  • • Indoor Monitor Cost: $300-$600
  • • Complete System (1 door + 1 monitor): $900-$3,000
Visual verification advantages:
  • Confirm identity before opening—face matches voice.
  • See if visitor is alone or accompanied.
  • Observe body language and demeanor.
  • Recorded evidence capture for security audit trails.
  • Package and delivery uniform verification.
  • Night vision capability for 24/7 security.
  • Passive monitoring of loitering outside the gate.

By Technology: Analog vs IP Network

Analog Intercoms

Traditional systems transmitting continuous electrical signals over dedicated wiring. Proven reliability but limited features.

  • Advantages: Proven reliability, simple troubleshooting, no network dependency, lower equipment cost.
  • Disadvantages: Limited scalability, standard definition video only, no remote/mobile access, difficult integration.

6. Audio Intercoms vs Video Intercoms

One of the first and most consequential decisions when selecting an intercom system is whether you need video capability. This choice affects cost, installation complexity, user experience, and security effectiveness.

Audio-Only Intercoms: Voice Communication

Audio intercoms transmit voice only. The door station has a microphone, speaker, and call button—no camera. The indoor station is either a handset (like a telephone) or a hands-free unit with speaker and microphone.

Component Costs:
  • • Door station (audio-only): $150-$400
  • • Indoor handset: $100-$300
  • • Complete system (1 door + 2 handsets): $400-$1,200

How Audio Intercoms Work:

Visitor presses the call button. Your indoor handset rings or chimes. You pick up the handset (or press the answer button on hands-free models) and speak. "Hello, who is it?" The visitor responds through the door station speaker. You have a voice conversation, decide whether to grant access, and press the door release button if appropriate.

The interaction is purely auditory—you hear the visitor's voice, they hear yours, but neither of you sees the other.

Advantages

  • Lower Cost (40-60% Savings): Significant cumulative savings across multiple gates. Audio at all three gates might cost $2,100, while video at all three costs $4,500.
  • Simpler Installation: Typically uses 2-wire cabling—a single twisted pair. Thinner, more flexible, and easier to route. Ideal for older building retrofits.
  • Higher Reliability: Fewer electronic components (no camera/IR/display) means fewer potential failure points. Typically lasts 15-20 years.
  • Sufficient for Known Visitors: Voice recognition of family, regular delivery staff, or domestic helpers is often adequate for residential security.
  • Works in Any Lighting: Audio is unaffected by lighting. Whether noon or pitch-black, communication clarity remains constant.
  • Privacy Benefits: No recording of visitor faces, reducing data protection obligations and PDPA compliance administrative burden.

Disadvantages

  • No Visual Verification: Critical limitation. Voice can be disguised or misrepresented. You miss cues like uniform, ID badges, or suspicious behavior.
  • Cannot Verify if Visitor is Alone: Accomplices can stand silently out of voice pickup range, waiting for the door to open. High risk for vulnerable residents.
  • No Evidence or Record: Provides no visual record for police or insurance if an incident occurs. Voices are insufficient as legal evidence.
  • Feels Outdated: Can reduce perceived property value and project a less professional security image for commercial buildings.
  • Limited Situational Awareness: Monitoring is only active during active calls. You cannot proactively check the gate area as you can with video.

Audio-Video Intercoms: Full Communication

Audio-video intercoms add a camera to the door station and a display screen to the indoor monitor. You see and speak with visitors simultaneously.

Component Costs:

  • • Indoor video monitor (7-inch screen): $300-$600
  • • Complete system (1 door + 1 monitor): $900-$3,000

Visitor presses call button. Your indoor monitor rings and the screen activates, displaying live video. You see the visitor's face, surroundings, and package legitimacy. You press answer and proceed with simultaneous video—observing actions and reactions while talking. Finally, you unlock the door or terminate the call.

Communication Architecture

7. One-Way vs Two-Way Communication

Not all intercom applications require full conversation capability. The communication direction—whether both parties can speak and hear each other, or communication flows only one way—fundamentally shapes the system design and appropriate use cases.

Two-Way Communication: Interactive Conversation

Two-way intercoms allow both parties to speak and hear each other, enabling real dialogue. This is the standard configuration for visitor vetting and most intercom applications.

How It Works:

Both the door station and indoor monitor have microphones and speakers. When connected, audio flows in both directions simultaneously (full-duplex) or alternating (half-duplex). You ask questions, the visitor responds, you follow up based on their answer—natural conversation.

Full-Duplex (Simultaneous)

Both parties can speak and hear at the same time, exactly like a telephone call. Modern IP intercoms and quality analog systems support full-duplex with digital echo cancellation.

  • • Natural, fluid user experience
  • • Allows interruptions & acknowledgments
  • • Industry standard for professional IP systems

Half-Duplex (Alternating)

Only one person can speak at a time. This is walkie-talkie style communication found in budget analog systems where echo cancellation is absent.

  • • Requires disciplined "speak-pause-listen" flow
  • • Cheaper but less efficient
  • • Common in legacy or lower-end equipment

Transmission Infrastructure

9. Traditional Analog vs IP Network Intercoms

The technological foundation of your intercom system—whether it uses traditional analog signaling or modern IP network architecture—fundamentally determines its capabilities, scalability, installation requirements, and future flexibility.

Analog Intercom Technology

Analog intercoms have been the standard since the 1960s, transmitting continuous electrical signals over dedicated wiring. They are essentially closed electrical circuits that translate sound waves into varying voltages.

The Signal Flow

Voice creates mechanical vibrations in the diaphragm, which are converted into analog voltage waveforms. These travel directly down the wire to the speaker with no digital processing or packetization.

[Waveform: Continuous Analog Path]

Wiring & Topology

Typically uses 4-wire or 2-wire "Star Topology" configurations. Each device must be hard-wired back to a central controller, creating labor-intensive expansion requirements.

[Diagram: Dedicated Star Wiring]

Advantage: Proven Reliability

Analog systems are the "utility" of the intercom world—functioning for decades with zero software dependencies. In Singapore’s tropical humidity, their simple circuitry offers inherent robustness against network outages or firmware glitches.

IP Network Intercom Technology

IP intercoms transmit digital data packets over standard CAT6 networks. They operate like computers on a network, using SIP (Session Initiation Protocol) to coordinate high-definition voice and video calls.

How Digital Communication Works

Voice and video are sampled, or "packetized," into digital data. These packets travel across standard network switches and are reassembled at the destination. This allows for **unlimited distance** and **superior HD resolution**.

  • PoE Efficiency: A single CAT6 cable carries both data and power.
  • Unlimited Scalability: Just plug a new station into an available network port.
  • Remote Management: Update firmware and monitor health across the entire estate remotely.

Access Control Integration

11. Door Release Mechanisms

The ability to remotely unlock doors, gates, and barriers is one of the most practical functions of an intercom system. Understanding how intercoms trigger locks and the various mechanisms available helps you design effective, reliable access control integration.

The Basic Principle: Relay Output

All intercom door release functionality is built on a simple electrical concept: the relay. A relay is an electrically operated switch. When you press the "door release" button on your intercom monitor, the intercom sends a low-voltage control signal to a relay inside the door station. The relay closes (completes a circuit), allowing electrical current to flow to the lock mechanism, causing it to unlock.

The Analog Switch

Think of it as a remote-controlled light switch. While you physically flip a regular switch, a relay "flips" electronically when you press the unlock button.

Voltage/Current Separation

Intercoms operate at low voltage (12-24V DC), but locks may require different voltages (up to 240V AC) and higher current (1-5 amps). The relay acts as a safe intermediary.

[Technical diagram showing relay inside door station: intercom button → low-voltage control signal → relay coil → relay contacts close → lock power circuit energized → door unlocks]

Relay Specifications and Wiring

Door station relays must handle the lock's electrical requirements specifically to avoid hardware failure.

NO (Normally Open)

Contacts are open by default. Energizes and closes current only when the release button is pressed. Most common configuration.

NC (Normally Closed)

Contacts are closed by default. Breaks the circuit when energized. Used for fail-safe locks that unlock when power is cut.

Changeover (C/COM)

Contains both NO and NC contacts on a single pole, providing flexibility for either wiring operation.

Pro Tip: Activation Duration

Most systems allow configurable "pulse" durations. For Electric Strikes, 1-3 seconds is typical. For Maglocks, 3-5 seconds ensures the person has time to push the door open. For Gates, 5-10 seconds is standard to accommodate slow-moving heavy barriers.

[Wiring diagram: door station relay connections: COM (common), NO (normally open), NC (normally closed) terminals with lock wiring]

Types of Lock Mechanisms

Intercoms can trigger various lock types, each with distinct characteristics and installation requirements.

Electromagnetic Locks (Maglocks)

An electromagnet on the frame holds a metal plate on the door with 600lbs to 1,200lbs of force. When power is removed, the field collapses instantly, unlocking the door.

  • Reliable: No moving parts to wear out.
  • Fail-Safe: Best for fire exits (always unlocks on power cut).
  • Glass Friendly: U-bracket mounting allows installation without drilling glass.
[Photo: Electromagnetic lock installed on glass door with U-bracket]

Electric Strikes (Strike Plates)

Replaces the standard strike plate in the frame. When energized, a solenoid pivots a keeper, releasing the latch bolt even if the handle wasn't turned.

  • Concealed: Appears like a standard strike plate from the outside.
  • Compatible: Works with existing door handles and hardware.
  • Fail-Secure Option: Can remain locked during a power failure for higher security.
[Cross-section diagram: Electric strike showing keeper position energized vs de-energized]

Electric Deadbolts

A motor-driven bolt that provides a 1-inch (25mm) throw into the frame. Much more secure than standard latch bolts.

  • High Security: Physically blocks the door with deadbolt strength.
  • Positive Locking: No dependency on holding force magnets.
  • Fail-Secure: Bolt remains extended when power fails.

Drop Bolts (Vertical Deadbolts)

A vertically-oriented deadbolt that extends into the floor. Essential for double doors with no middle post or narrow aluminum-framed doors.

  • Double-Door Solution: No center post (astragal) needed.
  • Mechanical Lock: Extremely difficult to force open.
[Diagram: Drop bolt installation at the bottom of double doors]

Motorized Gate Operators

For vehicle gates and pedestrian swing gates, intercoms trigger the motor control board via a "dry contact" relay output.

Integration Method

The intercom relay simply closes the circuit across the gate motor's "trigger" input. No power is supplied by the intercom; it just sends the "open" command.

Safety Requirement

Motorized gates must have safety sensors (infrared beams or edge sensors) to prevent the gate from closing on vehicles or people during operation.

[Wiring schematic: intercom relay connected to gate motor trigger terminals]

Manual vs. Automatic Door Release

How the visitor actually gains entry depends on the system workflow configuration.

Method Workflow Ideal Use Case
Manual Release User answers → Verifies identity → Presses "unlock". Unknown visitors, high-security sites, residential homes.
Automatic Release User answers → Door unlocks immediately. Pre-screened visitors, receptions, gated communites with security.

Door Position Sensors (Monitoring)

Advanced systems use magnetic contact sensors to monitor whether a door is actually open or closed. This adds a critical layer of security intelligence.

  • Alarm on Forced Entry: Triggers alerts if the door is opened without a valid unlock command.
  • Door Held Open Alert: Notifies management if a door is propped open for too long (e.g., >30 seconds).
  • Status Dashboard: Real-time visibility (Green=Closed, Yellow=Authorized Open, Red=Forced).
[Diagram: Door with magnetic contact sensor wired into intercom auxiliary input]

Fail-Safe vs. Fail-Secure (The Regulatory View)

This is the most critical distinction for safety and legal compliance in Singapore.

Fail-Safe (Life Safety)

Unlocks when power is removed. Required on fire exits to ensure people are never trapped during a disaster. **Example: Maglocks.**

Fail-Secure (Security)

Remains locked when power is removed. Appropriate for high-security internal rooms. **Example: Electric Deadbolts.**

Singapore Regulatory Note:

BCA codes mandate that emergency egress routes must be **Fail-Safe**. If a fail-secure lock is used, a mechanical manual override (like a panic bar) is legally required on the exit side.

[Comparison: Fail-safe maglock vs fail-secure strike during power failure]

Integration Wiring Best Practices

Proper wiring prevents voltage drops and hardware interference that could lead to intermittent failures.

  • Wire Gauge: Use 18-16 AWG for runs between 15-50 meters to avoid voltage drops.
  • Shielded Cable: Mandatory in industrial environments to prevent electromagnetic interference from triggering false unlocks.
  • Flyback Diodes: Install across DC lock terminals to protect relay contacts from back-EMF voltage spikes.
[Wiring diagram: Proper relay protection with flyback diode on maglock terminals]

Relay Output for Multiple Locks

A single door station may need to control multiple locks simultaneously—for example, a double-door entrance where both leaves must unlock together, or a scenario where a pedestrian gate and a main door both need to release.

Parallel Wiring

If both locks share the same voltage and the total current is within the relay's rating, they can be wired in parallel. For example, a 3A relay can easily handle two 400mA maglocks (800mA total).

Relay Expansion Modules

If the current draw exceeds the relay rating or the locks require different voltages, a relay expansion module is used. The primary intercom relay triggers the module, which then switches multiple secondary circuits.

Common Troubleshooting Guide

Problem: Door unlocks randomly.
Cause: Interference or short circuits. Solution: Switch to shielded cable and check for water ingress.

Problem: Relay clicks but door stays locked.
Cause: Voltage drop or failed coil. Solution: Measure voltage at the lock during activation (should be ±10% of rating).

Problem: Unlock works at monitor but not mobile app.
Cause: Cloud authentication or network settings. Solution: Verify cloud control is enabled in the station configuration.

Internal Communication

12. Multi-Station Intercalling

While most people think of intercoms primarily as visitor communication devices, many systems support internal station-to-station calling—the ability for one indoor monitor or handset to call another monitor within the same property. This internal communication capability is valuable for large homes, offices, facilities, and any environment where people in different locations need to communicate quickly without using mobile phones or walking to find each other.

What is Multi-Station Intercalling?

Multi-station intercalling is the ability for intercom stations within a property to call each other directly, independent of door stations. It creates an internal communication network similar to a telephone extension system, but using the intercom infrastructure.

Example scenarios:

Residential:

  • • Master bedroom monitor calls kitchen monitor: "Dinner is ready, please come upstairs."
  • • Living room monitor calls children's bedroom monitors: "Time to finish homework and get ready for bed."
  • • Parents' room calls maid's quarters: "Could you help with something in the living room?"

Office:

  • • Reception monitor calls manager's office monitor: "Your 3 PM appointment has arrived."
  • • Conference room monitor calls IT department: "We need technical assistance with the projector."
  • • Manager's office calls warehouse: "Is the shipment ready for pickup?"

Institutional:

  • • Hospital nurse station calls patient rooms: "Mr. Chen, it's time for your medication."
  • • School office calls classrooms: "Teacher, please send Sarah to the principal's office."
  • • Security control room calls remote guard posts: "Check the perimeter near gate 3."

The key distinction: These are internal calls between indoor stations, not visitor calls from door stations.

Master Stations vs. Substations

Multi-station intercom systems typically use a hierarchical structure:

Master Station (Control Station):

The master station has full calling privileges and enhanced features:

Capabilities:

  • Call any substation: Can initiate calls to any other station in the system
  • Call all stations (broadcast): Simultaneously call or page all substations
  • Call groups: Call multiple specific stations (e.g., all bedroom monitors)
  • Receive calls: Accept incoming calls from substations and door stations
  • Override/monitor: Some systems allow masters to listen to substations without alerting them (controversial privacy feature, used in healthcare or security applications with proper disclosure)

Typical locations:

  • • Parents' master bedroom (residential)
  • • Reception desk or manager's office (commercial)
  • • Nurse station or control room (institutional)
  • • Security headquarters (facilities)

Physical characteristics:

  • • Larger monitors with more buttons and controls
  • • Touchscreen directory showing all stations
  • • Physical buttons for frequently-called stations
  • • More sophisticated audio (better speakers, noise cancellation)

Substation (Extension Station):

Substations have limited calling capabilities:

Capabilities:

  • Call master station: Can always call designated master(s)
  • Call door stations: Answer door calls when visitor presses button
  • Limited or no substation-to-substation calling: Depending on system configuration, may or may not be able to call other substations directly
  • Receive calls: Accept calls from master stations, door stations, and (if permitted) other substations
  • Cannot broadcast: No ability to page all stations simultaneously

Typical locations:

  • • Children's bedrooms, guest rooms (residential)
  • • Individual offices, departments (commercial)
  • • Patient rooms, waiting areas (institutional)
  • • Remote guard posts, service areas (facilities)

Physical characteristics:

  • • Standard monitors or handsets
  • • Simplified button layout (answer, call master, door release)
  • • May lack directory or have limited directory

Why this hierarchy?

Security & Control

Prevents unauthorized users (children, visitors, staff) from calling all stations indiscriminately. Only authorized personnel have master privileges.

Simplified Operation

Substations have fewer options, making them easier for untrained users. "Press this to call the front desk" is simpler than a 50-name directory.

Cost Optimization

Master stations are more expensive ($400-$800). Substations are less expensive ($200-$400). reserve masters for key points to save budget.

Calling Methods and User Interfaces

How users select which station to call varies by system sophistication:

Direct Station Buttons (Simple Systems):

Each station has dedicated physical buttons for calling specific other stations. Example: Small office with 4 stations. Reception monitor has 3 buttons labeled: "Manager," "Conference Room," "Warehouse". Manager monitor has 2 buttons: "Reception," "Conference Room". Conference room has buttons for Reception and Manager. Warehouse has button for Reception only. Pressing a button initiates call to that station.

Advantages: Simple: No directory navigation, no typing—just press labeled button; Fast: Single button press to call; Intuitive: Physical labels clearly indicate who you're calling.

Disadvantages: Limited scalability: Only practical for 3-10 stations (too many buttons becomes overwhelming); Fixed configuration: Adding station requires rewiring or reprogramming buttons; No flexibility: Can't call station that doesn't have a dedicated button.

Touchscreen Directory (Modern Systems):

Touchscreen monitors display scrollable list of all stations. User workflow: Touch "Intercom" button to access internal calling; Directory appears showing all available stations with names/labels; Scroll through list or search by name; Touch desired station name; Call initiates.

Advantages: Highly scalable: Supports hundreds of stations in directory; Flexible: Adding stations updates directory automatically (software-based); Searchable: Find station by typing first few letters of name; Visual: Can display photos or icons representing each station.

Disadvantages: Slower: Requires navigating menu (multiple touches vs. single button); Touchscreen dependency: If screen fails, internal calling may be unavailable; Learning curve: New users must learn menu navigation.

Numeric Station IDs (Traditional Systems):

Each station has a number (1-99 or 101-199). To call a station, enter its number on keypad. Example: Master bedroom = Station 1; Kitchen = Station 2; Children's room 1 = Station 3; Children's room 2 = Station 4. To call kitchen from master bedroom: Press "2" then "Call" button.

Advantages: Universal method: Works on any monitor regardless of screen capability; Scalable: Supports up to 99 stations with 2-digit IDs; Simple hardware: Just needs numeric keypad, no touchscreen.

Disadvantages: Must memorize numbers: Users need to remember station IDs or keep reference chart; Error-prone: Easy to dial wrong number (especially for infrequent users); Not intuitive: Station "14" doesn't tell you it's the warehouse.

Speed Dial / Favorites (Hybrid Approach):

Modern systems combine methods: touchscreen directory for all stations PLUS configurable speed-dial buttons for frequently-called locations. Example: 3 programmable buttons on monitor home screen labeled "Kitchen," "Reception," "Security"; Additional "More" button opens full directory for less-frequent calls. Best of both worlds: Fast access for common calls, full flexibility for occasional calls.

Calling Modes: Who Can Call Whom?

System configuration determines calling permissions:

Unrestricted Calling (All-to-All):

Any station can call any other station directly. Architecture: Full mesh communication—every station is equal, no restrictions. When appropriate: Small offices where all staff are peers (no hierarchy); Residential homes where all family members should communicate freely; Collaborative environments emphasizing open communication.

Advantages: Maximum flexibility: Anyone can reach anyone; No bottlenecks: Don't need to route through central station.

Disadvantages: Potential misuse: Children calling each other at night, staff making personal calls during work; Privacy concerns: Anyone can call into any room; No access control: Cannot restrict certain areas.

Master-Substation Only (Hub and Spoke):

Substations can only call master stations. Substations cannot call each other directly. Masters can call any station. Architecture: Star topology with master at center. When appropriate: Hospitals (patient rooms call nurse station, not other patient rooms); Hotels (guest rooms call front desk, not other guest rooms); Homes with children (kids can call parents' room, cannot call each other's rooms after bedtime); Secure facilities (remote stations call control room, not each other).

Advantages: Controlled communication: All calls route through authorized station; Privacy protection: Prevents unwanted calls between substations; Supervision: Masters can monitor and manage all internal communication; Security: Prevents coordination between unauthorized users.

Disadvantages: Master bottleneck: If master station fails, substation-to-substation communication impossible; Less efficient: Must route through master even for simple substation-to-substation needs.

Selective Calling (Group-Based Permissions):

Stations are organized into groups. Calling permissions defined by group membership. Example: Office building; Group 1 (Management): Manager's office, executive conference room, Can call: Any station in any group; Group 2 (Operations): Reception, customer service, sales floor, Can call: Any station in Group 2, can call Group 1 masters, cannot call Group 3; Group 3 (Secure Areas): IT server room, finance office, CEO office, Can call: Any station in Group 3, can call Group 1 masters, cannot call Group 2. This creates flexible permissions matching organizational structure and security requirements.

Advantages: Granular control: Precisely define who can communicate with whom; Scalable: Works for large organizations with complex hierarchies; Security: Restrict access to sensitive areas; Flexibility: Adjust permissions as organization changes.

Disadvantages: Configuration complexity: Requires careful planning and ongoing management; User confusion: "Why can't I call the warehouse?" (permissions not obvious to users); Administration overhead: Changes require updating group memberships.

[Diagram showing three calling architectures: unrestricted (full mesh), master-substation (star), and group-based (clustered with permission boundaries)]

Group Calling (Calling Multiple Stations)

All-Call / Broadcast: Master station simultaneously pages all substations. How it works: Master presses "All Call" button; All substation monitors activate (speaker turns on, screen lights up); Master speaks, message broadcasts to all stations; Substations hear announcement but cannot respond (one-way broadcast). When used: Emergency announcements ("Fire alarm test in 5 minutes"); General notices ("Building closes in 30 minutes"); Family announcements ("Dinner is ready, everyone come downstairs"); Shift changes (factories paging all workers).

Group Call (Selected Multiple Stations): Call specific subset of stations simultaneously. Example: Manager wants to speak with all department heads. Presses "Department Heads" group call button. First-answer priority: All selected stations ring; First person to answer connects to master; Other stations stop ringing; Conversation is one-to-one (not conference call). When used: Reaching anyone from a group (don't care who answers, just need someone from that team); Urgent messages where multiple people should be available; On-call scenarios (call all on-duty staff, whoever answers responds).

Conference Call (Multiple Stations Connected Simultaneously): Advanced systems support true multi-party conferences where 3+ stations all hear each other. Example: Manager in office, warehouse supervisor, and loading dock staff all need to coordinate shipment. All three can speak and hear each other simultaneously. Requirements: Sophisticated audio mixing (combining multiple audio streams); Echo cancellation (preventing feedback loops); Higher bandwidth (multiple video streams if video conference). When used: Team coordination meetings; Emergency response (multiple responders coordinating); Complex problem-solving requiring input from multiple locations.

IP Intercoms and Network-Based Intercalling

Modern IP intercom systems bring sophisticated intercalling to accessible price points. All monitors are SIP devices on the network. Intercalling is simply SIP calls between monitors (same protocol used for visitor calls): User workflow: User selects "Intercom" on touchscreen; Directory of all stations appears (pulled from cloud database or local server); User selects station to call; Monitor sends SIP INVITE packet to target station's IP address; Target station rings, if answered, audio/video stream establishes (peer-to-peer or through SIP server).

Advantages over traditional analog intercalling:

  • Unlimited capacity: No hard limits on number of stations. Hundreds or thousands of monitors can be part of intercall network.
  • Dynamic directory: Add new station, it automatically appears in everyone's directory. Remove station, it disappears. No reprogramming.
  • Advanced routing: Call forwarding (calls to desk monitor forward to mobile app if desk monitor doesn't answer); Do-not-disturb mode (station doesn't ring during scheduled quiet hours); Presence indicators (busy, available, away status shown in directory); Call transfer (answer call, realize wrong person, transfer to correct station).
  • Geographic independence: Stations can be in same building or across the world. IP network connects them seamlessly.
  • Multi-modal: Same directory includes desk monitors, mobile apps, IP desk phones (if integrated). Call "John in Marketing" reaches him whether he's at desk monitor, on mobile app, or at desk phone.
  • Programmable: Intercalling rules, permissions, groups all configured via software interface (web portal). Changes take effect immediately without touching hardware.

Residential Homes (Large Properties):

Offices (Multiple Departments):

Teachers, please prepare your classes."; Daily operations: Cafeteria calls classrooms sequentially (selective group calling): "Grade 1 classes, lunch is ready. Hospitals (Patient Communication):

Scenario: Hospital ward, 40 patient beds, 2 nurse stations, pharmacy. Common intercalling uses: Patient calls: Patient in Room 312 presses bedside call button, rings Nurse Station A. Nurse answers via intercom: "Mrs. Lee, how can I help you?". Patient: "I need assistance getting to the bathroom.". Privacy, Etiquette & Costs:

Finally, multi-station setups must account for the human elements of communication.

Monitoring Ethics: Master stations in some systems can selectively "listen" to substations without ringing the station first. While legitimate for childcare or healthcare, this capability constitutes a privacy violation in many other contexts. Engineering best practice (and PDPA compliance) mandates disabling this feature except where essential, and always ensuring stations have visual indicators (LEDs) when their microphone is active.

Do-Not-Disturb (DND): Essential for bedrooms and meeting rooms to prevent intercalls during quiet hours or focused work. Systems should allow users to easily enable/disable internal ringing, with emergency overrides configured only for fire or security alerts.

Communication Etiquette: Direct intercalling is intrusive. Establish guidelines for use (e.g., "Use intercom for urgent house coordination; use mobile phone for social chatting"). Replaces the shouting through hallways that often occurs in larger Singapore multi-generational homes.
