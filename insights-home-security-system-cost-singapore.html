How Much Does a Home Security System Cost in Singapore?

The honest answer is: it depends on what you are trying to protect, how many entry points you have, and what level of response you want when something happens. This article gives you the actual numbers — and explains what drives them.

Founder & CEO · Securevision · 2026 · 7 min read

1. Why there is no single price — and what actually drives cost

A home security system is not a product you buy off a shelf. It is a set of components specified for your particular property — your floor plan, your entry points, your risk concerns, and how you want to be notified and respond when something happens.

The cost of a security system for a 3-room HDB flat and the cost for a detached bungalow on a corner lot are not comparable. What they share is a structure: you pay for hardware, you pay for installation, and for some services you pay an ongoing monthly or annual fee. Understanding each of those layers is what lets you compare quotes intelligently.

The four main systems homeowners install are burglar alarms, CCTV cameras, door access, and video intercoms. Most homes use one or two of these. Some use all four. The cost ranges below are for Singapore installations in 2025–2026, including equipment and professional installation.

2. Burglar alarm systems

A burglar alarm system comprises a controller, one or more keypads, detectors and sensors on doors and windows, motion sensors, a siren, and a communication module.

Typical installed cost ranges:

  • A basic wired system for a 3 to 4-room HDB flat — covering the front door, windows, and one or two interior motion sensors — runs from $800 to $1,800 installed. This gives you perimeter protection, a siren, and either self-monitoring via app or a monitoring centre connection.
  • A wireless system for the same property — using AJAX or RISCO SecuPlace — costs slightly more at $1,200 to $2,000 but requires no cable-chasing, which makes it cleaner for furnished homes and retrofits.
  • A wired system for a landed property — semi-detached or terrace, two to three storeys, covering all external doors, windows, and interior zones — typically runs $2,000 to $4,000 installed depending on the number of zones and the panel specified.
  • A larger bungalow or corner terrace with more entry points, a long perimeter, and central monitoring adds to that: $3,500 to $6,000 is a reasonable range for a comprehensive installation.
COST DRIVERS

What pushes cost up: number of zones (each detector is one zone), wireless vs wired, the communication module specified (IP only vs dual-path IP + GSM), and whether you add central monitoring.

Central monitoring (CAMS): If you connect to a professional monitoring centre such as Chubb Security, expect an additional $30 to $60 per month depending on the plan and response level.

3. CCTV camera systems

A CCTV system comprises IP cameras, a PoE network switch, an NVR (Network Video Recorder), hard disk storage, and cabling.

Typical installed cost ranges:

  • A 4-camera IP system for an HDB flat or small terrace — covering the front door, back door, driveway, and one interior zone — runs $1,200 to $2,000 installed. This typically uses Hikvision cameras and a 4-channel NVR with a 2TB surveillance-grade hard disk, giving you 20 to 30 days of continuous recording.
  • An 8-camera system for a landed home — covering all external perimeters, the driveway, and key interior points — runs $2,500 to $4,500 depending on camera specification. Adding AI analytics cameras (human/vehicle detection, intrusion zone alerts) pushes toward the higher end.
  • A system for a larger property with 12 to 16 cameras and a rack-mounted NVR runs $5,000 to $9,000 installed.
SPECIFICATION FACTORS

What pushes cost up: number of cameras, camera specification (standard vs AI analytics vs panoramic), hard disk capacity (more cameras or longer retention requires more storage), and whether the NVR is rack-mounted.

Cloud storage: Some homeowners opt for cameras with cloud backup in addition to local NVR recording. Cloud storage plans vary by provider and retention period — typically $10 to $30 per camera per month for 30-day cloud retention.

4. Video intercom systems

A video intercom for a landed home comprises a door station at the gate, one or more indoor monitors or app-connected units, and cabling between them.

Typical installed cost ranges:

  • A basic audio-only intercom for a HDB or condominium unit — replacing or upgrading an existing handset — runs $300 to $600 installed.
  • A video intercom for a landed home with one door station and one indoor monitor runs $800 to $1,500 installed for a traditional wired system (Aiphone or equivalent). Adding a second indoor monitor adds $200 to $400.
  • An IP-based video intercom with smartphone integration — such as the Akuvox R20A — runs $1,200 to $2,200 installed for a single door station, and gives you remote gate control and visitor image capture from anywhere with an internet connection.

5. Door access systems

A standalone door access system for a single door — card reader, electric lock, exit button, power supply — runs $500 to $1,200 installed depending on the reader technology (card only, card + PIN, card + fingerprint) and the lock type.

A digital door lock replacing a mechanical lock on a main door runs $400 to $900 supplied and installed, depending on brand and features.

For most private residential properties, standalone door access or a digital lock is the correct specification rather than a full networked access system.

6. Putting it together — realistic total budgets

Most homeowners do not install all four systems at once. Here is how typical total budgets look for different property types:

HDB flat (3 to 5-room):

  • Burglar alarm + basic CCTV (4 cameras): $1,800 to $3,500
  • Adding video intercom: +$600 to $1,200

Landed terrace or semi-detached:

  • Burglar alarm + CCTV (8 cameras) + video intercom: $5,500 to $9,000
  • Adding digital lock and standalone access: +$600 to $1,200

Larger landed (detached / bungalow):

  • Full integrated system — alarm, 12+ cameras, IP intercom, door access: $10,000 to $18,000

These ranges assume professional installation by a licensed security integrator. DIY camera systems cost less but provide no professional design, no warranty support, and no integration between components.

Typical budget ranges for different security subsystem combinations.

7. What the quote should include — and what to watch for

A professional security system quote should itemise: each hardware component with model number, installation labour, cabling, testing and commissioning, and warranty terms. If a quote is a single lump sum with no itemisation, ask for the breakdown before signing.

Watch for quotes that specify no-name or unbranded cameras and panels. The price may look attractive but the components are typically not supported by a local distributor, have no warranty backing, and cannot be serviced or expanded by another contractor if the original installer is unavailable.

The cheapest quote is rarely the right one. The right one is the one that specifies the correct system for your property, from a licensed installer who will still be reachable in five years.
