How to Use Your Burglar Alarm Correctly

Most alarm systems in Singapore are set up correctly and used incorrectly. The gap between how the system was designed to work and how people actually use it is where most false alarms and missed responses come from.

1. The keypad is your control point

Think of the keypad as the dashboard of your security system. It is the primary way you communicate with the controller. Modern systems like AJAX or RISCO have moved many functions to mobile apps, but the physical keypad remains the most reliable point of control for daily use.

Most keypads use a series of beeps to tell you the status. Understanding these sounds is critical. A slow, steady beep usually indicates the entry delay — the system has detected you and is waiting for your code. A rapid, frantic beep often means the system is about to trigger an alarm. If you hear these sounds, your first priority should always be the keypad, not checking the rooms.

2. Full Arming vs. Partial Arming (Stay Mode)

This is the most underutilised feature in residential security in Singapore. Many owners only "Arm" the system when they leave the house completely. This leaves them unprotected while they are inside.

  • Full Arm (Away Mode): Every detector in the house is active. Motion sensors in the living room, kitchen, and corridors are all protecting the space. This is for when the property is empty.
  • Partial Arm (Stay/Night Mode): Only the perimeter is active. Magnetic contacts on the doors and windows are armed, but the motion sensors in the corridors and living rooms are bypassed so you can move around freely inside.

Correct usage means arming the perimeter (Stay Mode) every single night. An alarm system that is only turned on when you are away is only protecting your belongings; an alarm system armed in Stay Mode is protecting your family.

3. Understanding Entry and Exit Delays

The exit delay gives you time to leave after arming. The entry delay gives you time to reach the keypad after opening the front door. These are typically set to 30 or 45 seconds.

The mistake people make is trying to "beat the clock." If you have bags or children and know it will take more than 30 seconds to reach the keypad, ask your installer to increase the delay to 60 seconds. It is far better to have a slightly longer entry delay than to have the siren trigger every time you come home with groceries.

USER TIP

If you trigger the alarm accidentally, do not panic. Enter your code immediately to silence the siren. Most monitoring stations or app-linked systems will register the "Alarm" followed immediately by a "Disarm by User," which tells them it was a false activation.

4. The Duress Code — use with caution

Most professional systems allow for a "Duress Code" or "Ambush Code." This is a secondary PIN (usually your normal PIN plus one digit) that disarms the system but simultaneously sends a silent "Emergency" signal to the monitoring station or your emergency contacts. This is specifically for situations where someone is forcing you to turn off the alarm.

Do not share this code with anyone who does not need to know it, and never use it to test the system without notifying your provider first.

Correct usage isn't about knowing every technical setting. It's about developing the habit of arming the system in the correct mode (Home vs. Away) every day. A system that is used consistently becomes second nature; a system that is used sporadically is the one that causes false alarms and frustration.
