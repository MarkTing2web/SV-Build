HDB, Landed, or Condo — How Security Requirements Differ

The security challenges of an HDB flat, a landed home, and a condominium unit in Singapore are genuinely different. The system that works well for one will be incomplete, over-specified, or simply wrong for another.

Wee Meng Ler
Founder & CEO · Securevision · 2026 · 7 min read
  • Home
  • Insights
  • Security Planning
  • Property Differences

1. Why property type shapes system design

Security system design starts with a site survey, not a product catalogue. The first thing any competent installer assesses is the property type — because property type determines entry points, perimeter characteristics, existing infrastructure, and what the occupant actually controls.

A homeowner in an HDB flat controls their front door and their unit. Everything outside that door is common property managed by the Town Council or MCST. A landed homeowner controls the entire plot — gate, perimeter walls, driveway, garden, all external doors and windows. A condominium unit owner is in a similar position to the HDB owner inside the unit, but in a building with more sophisticated shared infrastructure.

These differences have direct consequences for what you can install, where you can install it, and what the system needs to cover.

2. HDB flats — what you control and what you do not

In an HDB flat, your security perimeter is essentially the front door, the service door if present, and your windows. The common corridor outside is Town Council territory — you cannot install cameras pointing into it, and you cannot attach anything to common walls without approval.

What makes sense for an HDB flat:

  • Burglar alarm: Covering the front door contact, window contacts, and interior motion sensors. A wireless system (like AJAX) works particularly well to avoid cable-chasing through finished walls.
  • Video intercom: Replaces the doorbell to vet visitors without opening the door. Smartphone integration allows remote access even while away.
  • CCTV: Internal cameras for monitoring. Corridor-facing cameras are strictly limited to your own unit's immediate entrance.
REGULATORY NOTICE

HDB residents should check HDB's guidelines on camera installation. Rules around common-area attachments and camera angles are strictly enforced.

3. Landed homes — the full perimeter challenge

A landed property gives the owner full control but also full responsibility for securing the entire plot. The perimeter is yours to protect.

Perimeter coverage:

A typical terrace or semi-detached home has multiple entry points on several levels. Effective security must cover all of them, not just the primary front entrance.

  • CCTV: External cameras covering the driveway, gates, passages, and especially the backyard (a frequent vulnerability). Night-vision capability is essential for unlit gardens.
  • Burglar alarm: Magnetic contacts on all external doors and ground-floor windows. Interior motion sensors on each floor, with partial-arm capability for night-time security.
  • Gate intercom & Auto gate: A video intercom at the front gate integrated with the driveway's auto-gate motor for secure, app-based visitor verification and access.

Strategic component placement for a landing home perimeter.

4. Condominium units — shared infrastructure vs unit responsibility

Common areas and lobby access are managed by the MCST. As a unit owner, your focus is on your front door and any windows accessible from balconies or adjacent units.

KEY POINT

Shared infrastructure (lobby cameras, guards) does NOT protect your unit interior. Your unit door and private balconies are your individual responsibility.

Unit-specific security:

  • Private alarm: A separate system for the front door and windows to provide absolute peace of mind regardless of the condo's external security quality.
  • Intercom upgrades: Handset upgrades typically require coordination with the MCST as they integrate into a building-wide network.

5. Side-by-side comparison

Feature HDB Flat Landed Home Condo Unit
Burglar alarm Door, windows, 1-2 PIR Full perimeter + all floors Front door + balcony windows
CCTV Internal & Entrance area External perimeter + Interior Interior & Entrance
Intercom Wireless door camera Gate station + indoor monitor MCST Shared System
Auto gate N/A Driveway gate motor N/A (Shared Gantry)

6. The renovation timing question

The best time to upgrade security is during a renovation, before walls are closed. Cable runs that are easily concealed during works become significantly more expensive as retrofits on finished surfaces.

We work across all three property types. If you aren't sure what applies to your situation, simply tell us your property type and your primary concerns — we'll advise what makes sense for your specific case before you book a site visit.
