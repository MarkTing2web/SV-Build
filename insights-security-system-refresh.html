Why Your Security System Needs a Refresh — Not Just a Repair

A break-in in the neighbourhood is an uncomfortable reminder. Not because your home is next — but because it prompts the question you had been putting off: is what I have still good enough?

Ler Wee Meng
Founder & CEO · Securevision · March 2026 · 6 min read

A break-in in the neighbourhood is an uncomfortable reminder. Not because your home is next — but because it prompts the question you had been putting off: is what I have still good enough?

This article expands on a post originally shared on LinkedIn.

1. The call that came after the news

In early 2026, news broke of a break-in at a landed property in the Upper Thomson area. Reports in the local press confirmed what many residents in the district already sensed — that a quiet, established neighbourhood had been reminded of its vulnerability.

Local press reports of the Upper Thomson break-in, early 2026, prompted renewed security awareness among residents in the area.

One of our existing customers lives nearby. Their home is a semi-detached property with a backyard that backs onto a more exposed area — less natural surveillance from neighbouring houses, more open than the front of the property. They called us shortly after the news broke.

For years, the main security challenge at the property had been wildlife. Monkeys from the nearby nature corridor are a regular presence in Upper Thomson, and the occasional visit to the backyard had been more amusing than alarming. But the break-in changed the frame. What had felt like a quiet, low-risk environment now felt like something worth examining more carefully.

They asked us to come down and take a look.

2. What the site survey found

The front of the property was reasonably well covered — existing cameras, a functioning alarm system, a video intercom at the gate. The perimeter facing the road had been addressed years earlier when the system was originally installed.

The backyard was a different situation. The rear of the property had no camera coverage at all. The existing system had been designed primarily for the front — the natural point of entry, the visible face of the house. The back had been considered lower risk at the time of installation, and nothing had changed since.

That original assessment was not wrong. It was simply made in a different context. The neighbourhood had changed. The perceived risk had changed. The original system had not.

The site survey also revealed a secondary issue: the existing cameras at the front were original to the installation — several years old, pre-AI, recording at lower resolution than current standards. Functional, but not capable of the detection and response behaviour that current cameras support.

KEY POINT

A site survey is not just about finding what is broken. It is about assessing whether what is working is still appropriate for how the property is used and what the current risk environment looks like. Those are different questions.

3. The solution — see it, light it, alert it

For the backyard, the requirement was clear: broad coverage, night capability, and an active response that does not depend on the homeowner checking their phone at the right moment.

We installed a Hikvision wide-angle camera covering the full 180-degree arc of the backyard. The camera runs Hikvision's AcuSense AI analytics at the camera level — meaning the detection and classification happens inside the camera itself, not on the NVR. The camera distinguishes between a person, a vehicle, and an animal in real time. A monkey in the backyard does not trigger the same response as a person crossing the perimeter line at 2am.

When the AI detects a human presence in the defined detection zone, three things happen simultaneously and automatically:

  • Lights activate. Flood lighting covers the backyard. An intruder who was counting on darkness loses that advantage immediately.
  • Siren sounds. The audible alert is local and immediate — not a notification to a monitoring centre that takes minutes to respond, but a deterrent that activates at the point of intrusion.
  • Two-way audio engages. The camera has a built-in speaker and microphone. The homeowner can speak directly to whoever is in the backyard through the mobile app, from anywhere. In most cases, that direct voice contact is enough to end the situation before it escalates.

The homeowner also receives a push notification with a live view simultaneously, so they can assess the situation in real time and decide whether to call for assistance.

4. The principle behind the solution

The technical solution is straightforward. The principle behind it is worth spelling out, because it applies to every property owner reading this.

Detection alone is not enough. A camera that records an intrusion and stores the footage is useful for investigation after the fact. It is not a deterrent. The moment of deterrence — the moment that stops the incident from happening — requires a response that the intruder experiences immediately: light, sound, voice.

The sequence matters: see it, light it, make noise, alert the owner. Each step increases the cost of proceeding for the intruder and increases the speed of response from the homeowner. The combination is significantly more effective than any single element alone.

The AI distinction between human and animal is what makes this practical in an area like Upper Thomson, where wildlife movement is a genuine daily occurrence. A system that false-alarms every time a monkey crosses the backyard will be switched off within a week. A system that triggers correctly — responding to humans, ignoring animals — will be trusted and left on.

5. The broader point — security as a living system

The more important takeaway from this job has nothing to do with the specific camera or the specific backyard.

The customer's original system was not a failure. It was correctly specified for the property and the risk environment at the time it was installed. What changed was the context — the neighbourhood, the incident nearby, the customer's own reassessment of what adequate protection meant to them.

Security systems are typically installed and then largely forgotten until something goes wrong. The alarm arms and disarms. The cameras record. The intercom rings. Everything appears to be working. But appearing to work and being fit for purpose are not the same thing.

Environments change. Risk perceptions change. Technology advances significantly — a camera installed five years ago cannot do what a current AI-equipped camera can do, and the gap is not trivial. The property itself changes: renovations open new access points, landscaping grows to obscure camera views, new outbuildings create blind spots.

We support a significant number of homes and businesses whose systems were installed years ago. Some of those systems are still entirely appropriate. Others would benefit from a review — not because they have failed, but because the context they were designed for no longer fully matches the context they are operating in.

We do not sell security reviews as a revenue exercise. We suggest them when a customer's situation has changed meaningfully — a new risk in the neighbourhood, a renovation that altered the property, a system that is ageing past the point where it can support current detection capabilities. If you have not had your system reviewed in three or more years, or if something in your environment has shifted, it is worth a conversation. Not because something is necessarily wrong — but because staying ahead of the question is always better than being prompted by the wrong kind of event.
