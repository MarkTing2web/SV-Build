How Do I Migrate from Analogue to IP CCTV Without Full Replacement?

A practical engineering guide to transitioning your Singapore property from analogue CCTV to IP without tearing out your cable infrastructure or replacing every camera at once.

Ler Wee Meng Founder & CEO · Securevision · 10 min read
  • Home
  • Insights
  • How Do I Migrate from Analogue to IP CCTV Without Full Replacement?

Why the Analogue-to-IP Question Matters Now

The majority of CCTV systems installed in Singapore before 2016 are analogue — coaxial cable runs, BNC connectors, and DVRs recording at D1 or 720p at best. These systems are not broken in the functional sense. Cameras record, footage is accessible, alarms trigger correctly. The problem is that they are increasingly unfit for purpose in a landscape where investigation quality, remote access reliability, and integration with access control or building management systems are standard expectations.

Full replacement — ripping out every camera, every cable run, every DVR — is the nuclear option. It is disruptive, expensive, and in most Singapore properties, unnecessary. The analogue cable infrastructure is almost always reusable. The question is how to bridge it intelligently into an IP architecture.

KEY POINTS

In most Singapore properties, 70 to 90 percent of the analogue cable infrastructure can be retained during an IP migration. The cable is not the problem — it is the cameras, the recorder, and the signal type that need to change.

Understanding What You Already Have

Before any migration decision is made, the existing infrastructure must be audited. The key variables are:

  • Cable type. RG59 coaxial is the most common analogue CCTV cable in Singapore properties. RG6 is less common but also in use. Both can carry HD signals using HDCVI or HDTVI technology. Twisted-pair cable (used in some older passive video balun installations) has different characteristics and limitations.
  • Cable run lengths. Analogue coax reliably carries HD signals up to approximately 500 metres on RG59 for HDCVI. IP over coax adapters typically work to 300 to 400 metres depending on the adapter quality and cable condition.
  • Camera positions and housing condition. Existing camera housing and brackets can usually be reused with a new camera module, saving installation time and preserving aesthetics in developments with strict A&A restrictions.
  • DVR and power infrastructure. The existing DVR is almost certainly being replaced. The power supply feeds — whether centralised in the server room or local at each camera position — need to be assessed for load capacity with new cameras.

Option 1: HDCVI / HDTVI — HD Over Coax

HDCVI technology allows for HD resolution over existing legacy coaxial cabling.

This is the most commonly recommended migration path for Singapore properties with functioning coaxial cable runs in good condition. HDCVI (developed by Dahua) and HDTVI (used by Hikvision under their Turbo HD branding) are technologies that transmit HD video — up to 4K in current generations — over standard coaxial cable using the same BNC connectors already installed.

What changes: cameras (replaced with HDCVI/HDTVI models), DVR (replaced with an HDCVI/HDTVI-capable DVR or hybrid recorder).

What stays: all coaxial cable runs, all cable trunking and conduit, all camera mounting brackets and housing, the existing power infrastructure in most cases.

The result: a system capable of 2MP to 8MP recording across all existing camera positions, with remote viewing, motion detection, and in current hardware generations, AcuSense analytics — all without a single new cable run.

This path is suitable for: landed residential, SME offices, shopfronts, strata commercial units, and light industrial premises where the cable plant is in good condition and camera positions do not need to change.

Option 2: IP Over Coax Adapters

Where a true IP system is required — for integration with an IP-based VMS, or for sites that need Power over Ethernet functionality from the recorder — IP over coax adapters (also known as Ethernet over coax or EoC adapters) can convert existing coaxial runs to carry Ethernet-standard IP signals.

These adapters are installed in pairs: one at the camera end, one at the DVR/NVR end. They effectively create an Ethernet link over the coaxial cable, allowing any standard IP camera to be connected.

Limitations to note: EoC adapters introduce a modest bandwidth ceiling — typically 100Mbps per run, which is sufficient for a single camera but constrains options if a single coax run feeds multiple points. They also add a small latency component and a failure point (the adapter itself) not present in a native IP installation. Power must still be provided at the camera end, either locally or via a separate PoE injector.

Option 3: Hybrid DVR/NVR

Hybrid units allow for a seamless transition between old and new camera technologies.

A hybrid DVR/NVR accepts both analogue/HDCVI camera inputs via BNC and IP camera inputs via the network. This makes it the most practical tool for a phased migration — allowing a property to run analogue and IP cameras simultaneously from a single recorder while cameras are progressively replaced over time.

For a Singapore building manager planning a two-year migration budget, the hybrid approach is typically the most financially rational path. Year one: replace the DVR with a hybrid unit and convert the highest-priority camera positions (entry points, carpark) to IP. Year two: migrate remaining positions as budget allows. Throughout, the system operates as a single unified platform with one interface and one storage pool.

KEY POINTS

A hybrid recorder allows analogue and IP cameras to coexist on a single platform. This is the most practical tool for budget-phased migrations and eliminates the disruption of a single cutover event.

Option 4: Full Cable Replacement (When It Is Warranted)

Full cable replacement — pulling new CAT6 or fibre alongside or in place of existing coax — is warranted in a specific set of circumstances, and only those circumstances:

  • The existing coaxial cable is damaged, degraded, or too short for current camera positions.
  • Renovation work has opened walls and ceilings, making cable replacement incidental rather than disruptive.
  • The site is being comprehensively redeveloped and a future-proof structured cabling infrastructure justifies the investment.
  • Camera positions are changing significantly, requiring new runs regardless of technology path.

Outside these scenarios, insisting on full cable replacement is not engineering discipline — it is over-specification. A well-executed HDCVI or hybrid migration over existing coax will serve most Singapore properties for another decade without a single new cable run.

Phasing the Migration: A Practical Sequence

Careful planning and phasing are essential for a successful migration with minimal downtime.

A structured migration follows a logical sequence regardless of the technology path chosen:

  1. Phase 1 — Infrastructure audit. Document every camera position, cable run length, cable type, and condition. Identify any runs that require replacement. Establish current DVR recording quality and storage.
  2. Phase 2 — Recorder replacement. Replace the DVR with either an HDCVI-capable recorder or a hybrid DVR/NVR. All existing cameras continue operating through this transition — there is no system downtime during recorder swap.
  3. Phase 3 — Priority camera replacement. Convert entry points, perimeter cameras, and high-risk zones first. These positions benefit most immediately from improved resolution and analytics.
  4. Phase 4 — Systematic camera rollout. Remaining positions are converted on a schedule aligned to budget cycles — annually, quarterly, or in response to camera failures. Failed analogue cameras are replaced with IP equivalents rather than like-for-like analogue.
  5. Phase 5 — Full IP completion. Once all cameras are IP, the system is fully migrated. Storage, remote access, and analytics capabilities are now at full IP specification.

Securevision Verdict

The analogue-to-IP migration question has a practical answer for almost every Singapore property: retain the cable, replace the cameras and recorder in phases, and choose HDCVI or a hybrid platform as your bridge technology. Full replacement is rarely necessary and almost never the most cost-effective path. What is always necessary is an accurate audit of your existing infrastructure before any recommendation is made. Without knowing your cable type, run lengths, and camera positions, no quote and no migration plan is worth the paper it is written on.
