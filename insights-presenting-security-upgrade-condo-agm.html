How to Present a Security Upgrade at Your Condo AGM — and Get It Approved

Residents don't vote against security. They vote against proposals they don't understand or don't trust. Here is how to present one they will approve.

LW
Ler Wee Meng Written by Ler Wee Meng, Founder, Securevision Pte Ltd · NUS Engineering · UOL Law · 37 years in security systems
  • Home
  • Insights

Every MCST committee member who has tried to get a security upgrade approved at an AGM has felt this moment: you know the system needs replacing, you have done your homework, you have a proposal from a reputable integrator — and then a resident raises their hand and asks why the sinking fund should pay for this.

The room shifts. Suddenly you are defending a line item instead of presenting a solution.

This happens not because residents are unreasonable. It happens because security upgrades are invisible until they fail. Nobody in the room has seen the footage that was too blurry to identify a suspect. Nobody has waited at the guard house while the gantry jammed and a queue formed behind them. The committee member sees the problem every day. The resident sees only the cost.

This guide gives you the language, the structure, and the preparation you need to present a security upgrade proposal that residents understand, trust, and vote yes to.

Before the AGM

1. Start With the Problem, Not the Solution

The most common mistake committee members make when presenting a security upgrade is leading with the solution. "We are proposing to replace our CCTV system with a 32-camera IP network." The moment you say that, residents hear one thing: money.

Start with the problem instead. Before any AGM, document your current security failures in writing. Not opinions — documented incidents and system limitations.

How many times in the past twelve months did the guard house log a visitor complaint about the gantry? How many times was CCTV footage requested for a police report, and how many times was that footage unusable because of resolution or retention issues? How many access cards have been reported lost or cloned? How many times did the intercom fail and residents could not reach the guard house?

Documenting operational failures is the first step toward a successful proposal.

These are not hypothetical risks. They are operational failures that have already happened. Written down, dated, and presented as a list, they become the case for change — before you have mentioned a single dollar figure.

If you do not have this documentation, start now. Ask your managing agent to compile a fault log for the past year. Ask the guard team to provide their incident register. Ask the current integrator for a maintenance history report. This takes two to three weeks and it is the most important preparation you can do before any upgrade proposal.

Understanding What You Are Proposing

2. Understand Three Key Business Cases

Committee members come to security upgrades with very different levels of technical knowledge. Neither position is a problem — because what you need to present at an AGM is not a technical briefing. It is a business case.

There are three things you need to understand well enough to explain to a room of residents.

What is failing and why it matters.

Your current system has specific weaknesses. Your integrator should be able to give you a written system health report that lists every component, its age, its current support status, and what happens when it fails. You do not need to understand the technical details. You need to be able to say: our CCTV cameras are eleven years old, the manufacturer no longer supports the software, and when a camera fails we cannot replace it with a compatible unit. That is a complete, credible explanation that any resident can understand.

What the upgrade will do differently.

Focus on the two or three outcomes that matter most to residents. Faster visitor entry at the guard house. Camera footage that is actually usable for police reports. A phone app that lets residents open the gate for their own visitors without calling the guard house. These outcomes are tangible. Residents vote for outcomes, not specifications.

Why now and not later.

Your answer needs to be specific. If the current system is end-of-life, say so — and explain that end-of-life means no spare parts and no manufacturer support, which means the next failure may not be repairable at any cost. If the estate has had a security incident in the past year, this is where you reference it — not to alarm residents, but to make the cost of inaction concrete.

Handling the Cost Objection

3. When Residents Ask About the Sinking Fund

This is the question that derails more security upgrade proposals than any other. Here is how to handle it effectively.

Avoid These Pitfalls:
  • Do not say "it is for your safety." Safety arguments are too abstract to overcome cost resistance.
  • Do not say "every condo is doing this." Peer pressure undermines your management credibility.
  • Do not say "it is not that expensive." Relative cost arguments are always a trap.

What to say instead:

  • Frame it as asset maintenance: The security system is part of the estate's infrastructure — like the lifts or the roof. When the lift needs replacing, nobody asks why the sinking fund should pay for it.
  • Frame it as a cost comparison: Compare what the estate currently spends on reactive maintenance for the old system versus the fixed cost of a new, warranted system.
  • Frame it as property value: Estate security is a visible factor in property value and rental appeal. A well-maintained gantry and modern intercom are selling points.
  • Frame it in terms of the sinking fund balance: If the fund is healthy, specify that it was built precisely for planned capital expenditure like this.
Direct, honest answers build trust with the residents.
What to Put in the Proposal Document

4. The Five Must-Haves for Your Proposal

Residents need a written proposal they can read before the meeting. It must contain:

  1. The problem statement: A concise summary (no more than two paragraphs) of the current failures and limitations.
  2. The proposed solution: Plain-language descriptions of outcomes. Include the integrator’s police licence number and BCA registration.
  3. The cost breakdown: A fixed-price figure. residents vote against ranges because they feel like blank cheques.
  4. The disruption plan: How long will works take? Which areas will be affected? Properly planned disruption signals competence.
  5. The post-installation support: Warranty periods, response times, and written service commitments.
A clear, well-structured document is your strongest tool for approval.
On the Day

5. How to Handle Questions You Cannot Answer

The worst thing you can do is attempt an answer you are not confident in. Uncertainty is highly detectable and undermines the entire proposal.

The right answer is direct: "That is a good question and I want to give you an accurate answer rather than a guess. I will get the answer from our integrator in writing and circulate it to all residents within five working days."

Brief your integrator beforehand. Ask them to be available by phone or attend in person to answer technical questions directly. A licensed professional speaking to a room of residents is a significant asset.

After the Vote

6. If the Proposal Does Not Pass the First Time

This is not the end; it is information. Listen carefully to objections. If cost was the issue, strengthen your data. If it was trust, invite the integrator to an open session before the next meeting.

Do not reduce the scope to win approval. A half-upgraded system often fails faster and creates more complaints. If the full upgrade is necessary, present the full upgrade again with adjusted financing if needed.

The Committee Member's Job Is Not to Know Everything. It Is to Ask the Right Questions.

You do not need to be a security expert. You need to understand your estate's current problems well enough to explain them clearly. You need a proposal that translates technical solutions into resident-facing outcomes.

If you are not sure where to start, the right first step is a site assessment. Not a sales call — an engineering assessment that tells you exactly what your estate has, what condition it is in, and what a realistic upgrade looks like.
