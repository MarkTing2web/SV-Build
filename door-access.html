Access Control Fundamentals

1. What Is a Door Access Control System?

Door access control systems are electronic security solutions that replace traditional mechanical keys with credentials like cards, fingerprints, or mobile phones. They give you complete control over who can enter your premises and when.

Why Keys Are Insecure

Traditional mechanical keys have fundamental security weaknesses that make them unsuitable for modern security needs.

  • ✓ Easy Duplication: Any key can be copied at a locksmith for $5-$10 in minutes. There's no way to know if your key has been duplicated. A disgruntled employee or contractor could make copies before returning the key.
  • ✓ Irrevocable Access: When an employee leaves or a key is lost, the only solution is to change the entire lock and issue new keys to everyone. This is expensive, time-consuming, and creates a window of vulnerability.
  • ✓ No Audit Trail: You have no way to know who entered your premises, when they entered, or which door they used. If something goes missing, you cannot investigate who had access.
  • ✓ No Time Restrictions: A key works 24/7. You cannot restrict access to office hours only, or prevent weekend access. Everyone with a key can enter anytime.
  • ✓ Key Management: As your organisation grows, key distribution becomes a logistical nightmare. Who has which keys? How many copies exist? Which locks do they open?
Mechanical keys are easy to duplicate and impossible to audit.

How Door Access Control Solves These Problems

Electronic access control eliminates every weakness of traditional keys through intelligent digital management.

  • ✓ Complete Audit Trail: Every door access is logged with timestamp, user name, and door location. You know exactly who entered where and when. If an incident occurs, you can review access logs to investigate.
  • ✓ Instant Credential Revocation: When an employee leaves, you disable their credential in seconds. The card, fingerprint, or mobile access becomes invalid immediately. No need to change locks or collect keys.
  • ✓ Time-based Access Restrictions: Grant access only during specific hours. Employees can access the office 9 AM to 6 PM Monday to Friday. Cleaning staff can access all areas 6 AM to 8 AM only.
  • ✓ No Authorised Duplication: Access cards cannot be duplicated without the proper encoding equipment and software. Biometric credentials (fingerprints, faces) are unique to each individual and cannot be copied.
  • ✓ Centralised Management: Add or remove users from a single interface. Set access levels for different user groups. Generate reports showing who has access to what. Manage everything from one software platform or mobile app.
  • ✓ Integration with Other Systems: Access control integrates with CCTV (video verification of who accessed), time attendance (automatic clock-in), visitor management (temporary QR codes), and building automation (turn off lights when everyone leaves).
Modern systems integrate live monitoring with instant revocation.

What Is an Access Control System?

An access control system consists of four core components working together:

Credentials

Credentials identify the person seeking access. This can be an access card, fingerprint, facial scan, PIN code, or mobile phone.

Readers

Readers scan the credential and send the data to the controller. Readers are mounted outside the door.

Controllers

Controllers are the "brain" of the system. They validate the credential against the database, check if access is permitted (correct access level, correct time), and send the unlock signal to the lock.

Locks

Locks are electrified locks that release when the controller sends the unlock signal. Common types include electromagnetic locks, electric strikes, and drop bolts.

Management Software

Management software allows administrators to add/remove users, set access levels, review access logs, and configure system settings. Software can be on-premise (installed on a local server) or cloud-based (accessed via web browser or mobile app).

The access control process happens in milliseconds: User presents credential → Reader scans → Controller validates → Lock releases → Door opens.

Modern Access Control Capabilities

Modern systems go far beyond simple door unlocking, offering intelligence and remote management.

Mobile Access

Allows employees to unlock doors using their smartphones via Bluetooth, NFC, or QR codes. No physical card needed.

Facial Recognition

Provides touchless, hands-free access. Simply look at the camera and the door unlocks. Popular post-COVID for hygiene reasons.

Cloud Management

Enables you to manage the entire system remotely from a mobile app. Grant temporary access to a visitor while you're travelling. Receive instant alerts when doors are forced open.

Multi-factor Authentication

Combines two or more credentials for high-security areas. For example, card + PIN, or card + fingerprint. Prevents unauthorised access even if a credential is stolen.

Integration with Building Systems

Creates automated workflows. Last person exits → Doors auto-lock, alarm arms, lights turn off, air conditioning reduces. Fire alarm activates → All doors unlock for emergency evacuation.

Visitor Management

Issues temporary credentials (QR codes, visitor cards) with automatic expiry. Visitors receive a QR code via email, scan it at the entrance, and gain time-limited access.

System Architecture

2. How Door Access Systems Work

Understanding how access control components work together helps you make informed decisions about system design, capabilities, and limitations.

The Five Core Components

Every access control system consists of five essential elements that work in sequence to grant or deny access.

1

Credentials (Your Identity)

Credentials identify who is requesting access. Modern access control supports multiple credential types:

  • Access Cards: The most common credential. A card contains a unique ID number. Types include EM (125kHz), MIFARE (13.56MHz), and high-security DESFire (encrypted). Available as cards, fobs, or wristbands.
  • Biometrics: Fingerprints provide secure authentication that cannot be shared. Modern 3D Facial Recognition is contactless and hygienic. Palm Vein recognition scans internal vein patterns for maximum security.
  • Mobile Access: Using smartphones via Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), NFC (tap-to-enter), or QR codes. Convenient as most users always carry their phones.
  • PIN Codes: Numeric codes entered on a keypad. Often used as a secondary factor (Card + PIN) for high-security areas.
A single system can support cards, biometrics, and mobile access simultaneously.
2

Readers (The Scanner)

Readers scan credentials and send data to the controller. Card readers use radio frequency (RF) to energise the card and read its ID number. Reading distances vary from 3-10cm for standard proximity cards to up to 2 metres for long-range vehicle access.

Biometric readers use optical, capacitive, or near-infrared sensors to capture unique physical traits. Multi-modal readers combine technologies (e.g., face + fingerprint + card) for flexibility and redundancy. Readers connect via prioritised wired connections (RS-485 or Wiegand) for maximum reliability.

Professional readers are typically wired for high reliability and 24/7 operation.
3

Controllers (The Brain)

Controllers are the intelligence of the system. They perform critical checks: Is the credential valid? Does this user have access to this door? Is the current time within permitted hours? If all pass, it sends an unlock signal and logs the event for the audit trail.

Network controllers connect to a central server or cloud platform, enabling real-time monitoring and centralised management of hundreds of doors. Standalone units act independently, ideal for single-door setups where networking is unfeasible.

Controllers manage the logic and hardware interface for 1 to 8 doors each.
4

Locks (The Physical Barrier)

Electrified locks secure the door and release on signal. Common types include:

  • EM Locks: Powerful magnets (300kg-1200kg). Fail-safe (unlocked without power), essential for fire safety.
  • Electric Strikes: Replaces the strike plate. Available in fail-safe and fail-secure (stays locked without power) versions.
  • Drop Bolts: Motorised bolts for high-security glass or double doors.
  • Electric Mortise: Integrated motorised latches for residential or hotel applications.
Lock selection depends on door type, fire safety, and security requirements.
5

Management Software

The interface for administrators to configure users, assign access levels, and generate audit reports. On-premise software keeps data locally (no subscription), while Cloud-based software enables management from any web browser or mobile app, popular for its ease of use and remote accessibility.

Software acts as the control panel for the entire security ecosystem.

System Architecture & Signal Flow

The complete access control sequence happens in milliseconds (200-500ms), ensuring a smooth user experience:

  • 1 Presentation: User presents credential (card, finger, face).
  • 2 Data Extraction: Reader scans and extracts the unique ID or template.
  • 3 Validation: Controller checks database permissions and time groups.
  • 4 Action: If authorised, the controller signals the lock to release.
  • 5 Audit: The event is logged (name, door, time) for tracking.
Every door authentication happens in milliseconds across these five critical components.

Standalone vs Networked vs Cloud Systems

Access control systems are deployed in three main architectures, each suited for different scales.

Standalone

Operates independently. Credentials programmed directly on the reader. Lowest cost, but no centralised management or comprehensive audit trail.

Network (On-Premise)

Controllers connected to a local server. Centralised management and detailed reporting. No internet required, but requires server maintenance.

Cloud-Based

Manage from anywhere via app or browser. No server needed, automatic updates. Highly scalable and ideal for multi-site businesses.

Access Modes & Authentication Methods

Choose the right security level for each area of your facility:

Single-Factor Auth

Card only or Biometric only. Fast and convenient. Suitable for general office areas and staff entrances.

Two-Factor Auth (2FA)

Card + PIN or Card + Biometric. Prevents unauthorised access if a card is stolen. Recommended for server rooms and finance areas.

Multi-Factor & Two-Person Rule

Maximum security vault areas may require Card + PIN + Biometric or the Two-Person Rule (requires two people to authenticate simultaneously).

Standalone & Small Systems

4. Single Door Access Control

Single door access control systems are self-contained reader-controller units ideal for controlling one to three doors without centralised management requirements.

What Is a Single Door System?

Unlike multi-door systems that separate readers from central controllers, single-door systems integrate everything in one device. The unit contains the scanner, the "brain" (logic), the user database, and the lock relay. It is mounted directly next to the door, eliminating the need for extensive cabling back to a server room.

Standalone vs Network-Capable

True Standalone Readers

Operate completely independently. Programmed via keypad or master card. No remote access or software. Best for side gates or very small offices with <10 users.

Network-Capable Units

Can operate standalone OR connect to WiFi/Cloud. Offers the flexibility to start small and add centralised management later as your organisation grows.

When Single Door Makes Sense

Single door systems are the right choice when:

  • ✓ 1-3 Doors Total
  • ✓ Fewer than 50 Users
  • ✓ No complex audit trail required
  • ✓ Budget-constrained projects
  • ✓ Temporary site installations

Scalable Systems

5. Multi-Door Access Control

Multi-door access control systems use separate controllers and readers to manage 2-250+ doors with centralised management, comprehensive audit trails, and deep integration capabilities.

What Is a Multi-Door System?

Multi-door systems separate the "intelligence" (controllers) from the "interface" (readers). This enables centralized management of an entire building from a single software platform.

  • Architecture: Readers are mounted outside doors as simple scanners, while controllers are installed in secure internal locations like electrical or IT rooms.
  • Wiring: Readers connect to controllers via RS-485 or Wiegand data cables. Controllers then link to your network via Ethernet.
  • Configurations: Controllers typically manage 2, 4, or 8 doors each. A system scales indefinitely by networking multiple controllers together.
Professional controllers are installed inside the protected area for maximum security.

Why Multi-Door Is More Secure

Internalised Intelligence

Unlike standalone readers, multi-door controllers are hidden inside locked server rooms. Even if an external reader is tampered with, the door remains locked as the decision-making logic is inaccessible from the outside.

Tamper Detection

Modern controllers can detect when a reader is removed or cabling is cut. They can trigger immediate alarms or notification alerts to your security team before a breach occurs.

Advanced Features & Logic

Multi-door platforms enable sophisticated security rules that are impossible with standalone units:

Anti-Passback

Prevents credential sharing and tailgating. A user must exit a zone before they can enter again, ensuring one card cannot be used by multiple people to enter.

Door Interlock (Mantrap)

Used for high-security airlocks where only one door can be open at a time. Door B will not unlock until Door A is fully closed and locked.

Access Levels (User Permissions)

Assign permissions based on roles. A "Staff" level might access office floors, while "Management" level includes server rooms and executive suites.

Event-Triggered Actions

Automation based on events. Examples: activate CCTV recording on a forced entry, or unlock all doors automatically if a fire alarm is triggered.

Integration Capabilities

Multi-door systems act as the backbone for unified building security:

CCTV Verification

Link access events to video. Pop up camera feeds when sensitive doors are accessed.

Time & Attendance

Use door entry/exit to track clock-in times and generate automated HR reports.

Lift/Elevator Control

Restrict elevator access by floor, ensuring users can only reach their authorised levels.

Retrofit & Migration

6. Upgrading from Mechanical Locks to Access Control

Most businesses already have doors secured with traditional mechanical locks and keys. Upgrading to electronic access control doesn't always require replacing doors or frames—in many cases, you can retrofit access control onto existing infrastructure.

Assessing Your Current Setup

Before upgrading, evaluate whether your existing doors are suitable for an access control retrofit. Most doors can accommodate electronic security, but material and frame condition affect the complexity.

Door Materials
  • Wood/Metal: Easiest to retrofit. Accepts most locks.
  • Aluminium: Common in commercial buildings. May require reinforcement for heavy EM locks.
  • Glass: Requires special U-brackets or patch fittings. Drilling is not possible.
  • UPVC/Composite: Requires careful drilling to avoid damaging the internal core.
Frame Condition

Frames must be solid to support the weight and force of electronic locks. Hollow frames may need internal reinforcement for high-holding-force EM locks (600kg+).

Retrofit vs New Installation

Option Advantages Best For
Retrofit Lower cost (30-40% savings), faster installation, minimal disruption. Doors in good condition, standard commercial frames.
New Installation Cleanest appearance, guaranteed alignment, upgrade to fire-rated doors. Damaged/warped doors, high-security vault or core upgrades.

Phased Implementation Approach

Phased upgrades help spread costs while providing immediate security benefits at your most vulnerable points.

Phase 1

Main Entrances

Immediately control who enters the building. Revoke ex-staff access instantly and track all visitors.

Phase 2

Secondary Access

Secure side doors, loading bays, and parking gates to prevent common tailgating or unauthorised entry points.

Phase 3

Restricted Internal Zones

Control internal movement for server rooms, finance departments, and executive suites where security is paramount.

Common Retrofit Challenges

No Power Near the Door?

Solution: We use Power over Ethernet (PoE) which delivers data and power over a single cable, or wireless battery-powered locks for low-traffic internal doors.

Glass Door Constraints?

Solution: U-bracket EM locks clamp onto the top of the glass, providing a powerful holding force without requiring any drilling in the tempered glass.

Security & Administration

7. Credential Security & Management Lifecycle

Effective credential management is critical to access control security. Understanding how credentials are issued, stored, and deactivated ensures your system remains secure throughout its lifecycle.

The Enrolment Process

Modern systems make user on-boarding fast and professional, typically taking only 2-5 minutes per user.

Card Enrolment

Associate a unique ID with an employee name in the software. Bulk imports from CSV are available for large organisations.

Biometric Templates

Fingerprint or facial scans create mathematical "templates"—one-way hashes that cannot be reversed into photos for privacy security.

Mobile Provisioning

Virtual credentials are sent via app or email. Employees self-enrol on their smartphones, reducing administrative overhead.

Storage & Encryption Security

Security in Transit

Communication between readers and controllers should be encrypted via OSDP (Open Supervised Device Protocol) to prevent credential interception or "sniffing".

Encryption at Rest

Credential databases on servers and in the cloud are encrypted using AES-256 standards, ensuring your user data is protected even in the event of hardware theft.

Instant Revocation: The Absolute Benefit

The primary advantage over mechanical keys is the ability to instantly revoke access. When an employee leaves, their credential is deactivated in the software in seconds, reflecting across all networked doors immediately.

Best Practice: Deactivate credentials during the exit interview process. Never wait until the end of the day or the following morning to secure your premises.

Hardware Selection

9. Reader Types Explained

Readers are the physical interface between users and the access control system. Choosing the right reader type depends on your chosen credential technology, security requirements, and environmental conditions.

RFID Card Readers

Card readers are the industry standard for commercial access. They scan cards or fobs and transmit the encrypted ID to the door controller. We typically categorise these by frequency:

  • EM Readers (125kHz): Basic, reliable, and low cost. Ideal for residential gates or internal office doors.
  • MIFARE/Smart Readers (13.56MHz): Support encrypted communication. Highly recommended for commercial suites to prevent card cloning.
  • Multi-Technology: Support both old EM and new MIFARE cards—perfect for phased facility upgrades.

Biometric & Facial Terminals

Biometric readers eliminate the risk of shared cards and "buddy punching" in time attendance systems.

Fingerprint Readers

Available as optical or capacitive sensors. Modern units store up to 5,000 templates locally and perform matching in under 1 second.

Facial Recognition Terminals

3D terminals use dual cameras for depth sensing, preventing spoofing via photos. Completely touchless and hygienic.

Environmental & Placement Considerations

IP66 Weatherproofing

Outdoor readers must be rated for Singapore's humidity and heavy rain. We use UV-resistant housings to prevent fading in direct sunlight.

Optimal Mounting Height

Standard readers are mounted 1.2m to 1.4m from the floor. Facial terminals are mounted slightly higher (1.4m to 1.6m) to align with adult face height.

Vandal Resistance

For public-facing gates, we recommend IK10 impact-rated readers with built-in tamper switches that alert security if the unit is pried off the wall.

Electrified Locks

10. Lock Types & Selection

The electric lock is the physical strength of your system. Choosing the right lock depends on the door material, swing direction, and fire safety requirements of your specific location.

Electromagnetic (EM) Locks

EM locks use powerful magnetic force to hold a door closed. They consist of an electromagnet on the frame and an armature plate on the door. They are inherently Fail-Safe (unlock when power is cut), making them the gold standard for fire exit routes.

  • 300kg/600lbs: Best for internal office doors.
  • 600kg/1200lbs: Industry standard for commercial entrances.
  • U-Brackets: Mandatory for frameless glass doors (no drilling required).

Alternative Lock Mechanisms

Electric Strikes

Replaces the standard strike plate in the frame. Allows the door to stay locked mechanically but release electronically. Best for wooden doors.

Electric Drop Bolts

A heavy-duty metal bolt that drops into a floor strike. Provides double-swing door compatibility and very high holding force.

Electric Mortise Locks

Integrated into the door itself. Provides the cleanest appearance (hidden) and includes a mechanical key override for emergencies.

Traditional systems rely on local servers. Cloud systems connect readers directly to platforms like AWS or Azure. Intelligence is moved from your server room to the cloud, allowing for true "anywhere" management.

This decentralized architecture eliminates the single point of failure and allows for real-time synchronization across global locations.

End-to-End Encryption

All communication between readers and the cloud uses TLS 1.2+ (military-grade) tunnels. Credentials are stored as encrypted hashes, useless to intruders.

Omnichannel Multi-Site Governance

Manage ten different branches from a single unified dashboard with one login. Centralised visibility allows for global policy enforcement and standardized reporting across all your locations.

Enterprise Governance: Unified monitoring of global sites from a single browser-based command centre.

Offline Intelligence: What if Internet Fails?

A common concern is internet reliability. Modern cloud readers are "edge-intelligent":

Local Caching

Readers store up to 10,000 recently used credentials in local memory. If the internet goes down, existing employees can still enter normally. Access decisions are made at the door.

Auto-Sync

Once the connection is restored, the reader automatically uploads all offline logs and downloads any new user changes made during the outage.

Enterprise Tip: For high-uptime requirements, we deploy readers with 4G/LTE failover SIM slots to ensure the system is manageable 24/7.

14. Access Levels, Time Groups & Permissions Architecture

Permissions are built on a layered architecture of Access Levels and Time Groups. This structure enables sophisticated security policies that scale effortlessly without manual per-user configuration.

Hierarchy of Permission

Instead of managing users individually, we use logical templates to automate security:

  • Access Levels: Templates that define WHERE a user can go (e.g., "Staff" = Main + Pantry + Meeting Rooms).
  • Time Groups: Schedules that define WHEN access is allowed (e.g., "Office Hours" = Mon-Fri, 08:00 - 18:00).
  • Door Groups: Logical zones that combine multiple doors (e.g., "Public Areas" = 5 separate lobby doors) for easier batch assignment.
Permissions architecture: Grouping readers into logical 'Access Levels' for rapid user on-boarding.

Instead of dedicated time-clocks, we configure exit and entry readers to function as attendance terminals. The "First entry of the day" is logged as the clock-in, and the "Last exit of the day" is the clock-out. The system automatically calculates total hours, excluding lunch breaks where required.

MOM Compliance: Under the Employment Act, Singapore employers must maintain accurate records of work hours (including overtime) for at least two years. Our systems automate this record-keeping for audit readiness.

Emergency Procedures

22. Fire Safety & Compliance

Access control systems must never impede emergency evacuation. In Singapore, SCDF fire codes mandate strict integration between security hardware and life safety systems.

SCDF Mandatory Compliance: Fail-Safe Requirement

All doors located in a "Means of Escape" (exit routes) MUST use Fail-Safe locks. This ensures that upon fire alarm activation or power failure, all magnetic locks release immediately to allow unimpeded evacuation. Fail-Secure locks are strictly prohibited on fire exit doors unless a mechanical manual override is provided.

Fire Alarm Integration

Our controllers are wired directly to the building's Fire Alarm Panel (FAP) via a dry-contact relay. When the alarm triggers, the 12/24V power to all door locks is cut at the source—guaranteeing that occupants can exit without fumbling for cards or fingerprints during an emergency.

Fire Safety Integration: Life-safety signals from the FAP always take priority over the security system.
Data Protection

23. PDPA, Privacy & Cybersecurity

As access control systems collect personal data (names, movements, and biometric templates), compliance with Singapore's Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) is essential.

PDPA Obligations

You must obtain explicit Consent before enrolling staff biometrics. Use Purpose Limitation notices to explain that data is for security and attendance only. Always implement a Retention Policy to auto-purge logs after 90–365 days.

Cyber Resilience

Access control management software should be on a separate VLAN, isolated from the corporate network. Always enforce MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication) for system administrators to prevent unauthorised user additions.

Lessons Learned

24. Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Under-specifying System Capacity

Buying a 2nd-floor controller with no spare capacity often results in expensive hardware replacement when the company grows by just one door. Always plan for 20% future expansion.

2. Incorrect Lock Selection for Outswing Doors

Installing a standard strike on an outswing door is a common site-survey error. Always verify the door swing before ordering hardware to avoid rework costs.

3. Neglecting "No-Drip" Loops for Outdoor Readers

For side-gate readers, rain can travel along the cable directly into the electronics. Always ensure a "drip loop" is made during installation to prevent water damage.

4. Weak Admin Passwords

Using default "admin/1234" login for the door controller is an open invitation for hackers. Change passwords immediately upon commissioning.

26. Frequently Asked Questions

What happens to my doors during a power failure?

This depends on your lock type. **Fail-Safe** doors (like EM locks) will unlock immediately for safety. **Fail-Secure** doors (like some electric strikes) will stay locked. We recommend installing a UPS (Battery Backup) to keep systems powered for 2–4 hours during an outage.

Can I use my existing door or must I replace the whole frame?

Most wood and metal doors can be retrofitted. We can install electric strikes into existing frames or mount EM locks to the top header without replacing the entire door structure.

Is cloud access control more expensive than on-premise?

Cloud systems have lower upfront costs (no server needed) but require a small monthly subscription. On-premise systems have a higher initial "CapEx" but minimal ongoing software costs. Cloud is usually 30% more cost-effective for 1-5 door setups.

How often should I audit my user database?

We recommend a full audit every quarter. This ensures that any temporary contractors or ex-employees are permanently purged from the system, maintaining your security integrity.
