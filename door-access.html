Fundamentals

1. What Is a Door Access Control System?

Door access control systems are electronic security solutions that replace traditional mechanical keys with credentials like cards, fingerprints, or mobile phones. They give you complete control over who can enter your premises and when.

Why Keys Are Insecure

Traditional mechanical keys have fundamental security weaknesses that make them unsuitable for modern security needs.

  • ✓ Easy Duplication: Any key can be copied at a locksmith for $5-$10 in minutes. There's no way to know if your key has been duplicated.
  • ✓ Irrevocable Access: When an employee leaves or a key is lost, the only solution is to change the entire lock.
  • ✓ No Audit Trail: You have no way to know who entered your premises, when they entered, or which door they used.
  • ✓ No Time Restrictions: A key works 24/7. You cannot restrict access to specific hours.
[Comparison image, split-screen: LEFT side shows messy key ring with 10+ traditional metal keys hanging, labeled "INSECURE" | RIGHT side shows single clean white RFID access card, labeled "SECURE"]
Mechanical keys are easy to duplicate and impossible to audit.

How Door Access Control Solves These Problems

Electronic access control eliminates every weakness of traditional keys through intelligent digital management.

Complete Audit Trail: Every door access is logged with timestamp, user name, and door location. You know exactly who entered where and when. If an incident occurs, you can review access logs to investigate.

Instant Credential Revocation: When an employee leaves, you disable their credential in seconds. The card, fingerprint, or mobile access becomes invalid immediately. No need to change locks or collect keys.

Time-based restrictions: Grant access only during specific hours. Employees can access the office 9 AM to 6 PM Monday to Friday. Cleaning staff can access all areas 6 AM to 8 AM only.

[System overview diagram showing flow: Credential → Reader → Controller → Lock]
Modern systems integrate live monitoring with instant revocation.

Core System Components

An access control system consists of four core components working together: Credentials (Identity), Readers (Scanners), Controllers (Brain), and Locks (Physical Barrier). These are managed via software that can be on-premise or cloud-based.

2. How Door Access Systems Work

Understanding the components and how they work together helps you make informed decisions about your security infrastructure.

The Five Core Components

1

Credentials (Your Identity)

Credentials identify who is requesting access. Modern access control supports multiple credential types:

  • Access Cards: RFID cards (EM, MIFARE, DESFire) or fobs.
  • Biometrics: Fingerprints, Facial Recognition, or Palm Vein.
  • Mobile Access: Unlocking via Smartphone (BLE/NFC/QR).
  • PIN Codes: Numeric codes entered on a keypad.
[Array of different credential types: RFID card, fingerprint, smartphone app, facial recognition terminal]
A single system can support cards, biometrics, and mobile access simultaneously.
2

Readers (The Scanner)

Readers scan credentials and send data to the controller. They use radio frequency (RF) to read cards or specialized sensors for biometrics. Readers connect to controllers via RS-485 or Wiegand protocol.

[Professional RFID card reader mounted on glass door frame]
3

Controllers (The Brain)

Controllers act as the intelligence. They make the critical decision: grant or deny access based on the database and rules. Network controllers enable real-time monitoring and centralized management.

[Controller circuit board in metal enclosure with wiring]
4

Locks (The Physical Barrier)

Electrified locks release when the signal is sent. Varieties include:

  • EM Locks: Magnetic holding force (300kg-1200kg). Fail-safe.
  • Electric Strikes: Fits into wood/metal frames. Fail-secure.
  • Drop Bolts: Motorized bolts for glass or double doors.
[EM lock installation on glass door top]
5

Management Software

The software interface used to add/remove users, set access levels, and generate audit reports. High-end systems integrate with CCTV for visual verification.

[Signal flow diagram showing sequence from User to Lock]

3. Types of Access Control Systems

Access control systems are categorized by deployment scale and control architecture.

Deployment Scale

Single Door

Self-contained reader-controller unit. Capacity: 3,000+ users. Best for small shops or residential side gates.

Multi-Door

Separate controllers managing 2-8 doors each. Centralized software management. Best for professional offices.

Enterprise

Manage hundreds of doors across sites. Unified platform for access, CCTV, and lift control.
