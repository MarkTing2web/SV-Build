What Are the MCST's Legal Obligations for Security Systems in Singapore?

Most MCST committees manage their estate's security by instinct and inherited practice. Here is what the law actually requires — and where the gaps can create real risk.

Ler Wee Meng Founder & CEO · 37+ Years Experience
Important Notice This article provides general information about Singapore's regulatory framework as it applies to security systems in residential estates. It is not legal advice. For specific legal questions about your estate's obligations, consult a qualified lawyer or your managing agent.

Most MCST committees in Singapore manage their estate's security the way it has always been managed — using the same systems the developer installed, serviced by whoever the previous committee appointed, with decisions made based on what seems reasonable rather than what the law requires.

Committee members are residents who stepped forward to help manage their community, and they are doing so in their spare time with limited technical support. But legal obligations do not adjust for good intentions.

Three regulatory frameworks directly affect how your estate's security systems must be installed, operated, and maintained. Understanding your obligations is not about discovering how much trouble you are in — it is about making informed decisions and protecting your estate from avoidable liability.

Framework One

1. PDPA — Managing CCTV and Resident Data

The Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) governs how organizations, including MCSTs, handle personal data. Faces on CCTV, vehicle plates at the gantry, and access logs all count as personal data.

Restricting access to footage to authorized personnel is a core PDPA requirement.

What the PDPA Requires:

  • Proper Purpose: You must have a stated, reasonable purpose for every camera placement (e.g., resident safety, incident investigation).
  • Restricted Access: Access to footage must be limited to authorized personnel only. If any guard can export footage to a USB without a log, you have a compliance gap.
  • Defined Retention: Footage should only be kept as long as necessary. The Singapore condo standard is typically 30 days unless related to an investigation.
  • Data Protection: Recording systems (NVRs) must be password protected and secure from unauthorized external access.
  • Documented Requests: There must be a clear process for handling requests for footage from residents or the police.

Ask your integrator: "Has our system been configured with role-based access controls, automatic retention policies, and access audit logging?"

Framework Two

2. BMSMA — Maintenance Obligations

The Building Maintenance and Strata Management Act (BMSMA) defines the MCST's responsibility for common property. Security systems in common areas are common property.

The MCST has a legal duty to maintain security infrastructure in good repair.

What the BMSMA Requires:

  • Duty to Repair: Section 29 places a duty on the MCST to keep common property in good repair. Neglecting security systems to the point of failure can be a breach of this duty.
  • Resolution Authority: Capital expenditure for security upgrades above a certain threshold requires a general meeting resolution (AGM/EGM).
  • Sinking Fund Use: The sinking fund is intended precisely for major repairs and replacement of common property, including security infrastructure.
  • Documented Maintenance: Proper records of all service visits and expenditure must be kept for auditing and compliance verification.

Ask your managing agent: "What is the current condition of our security systems, and is there any deferred maintenance that creates a compliance risk?"

Framework Three

3. SCDF Fire Code — Access Integration

The SCDF Fire Code requires that any electronic locking device (like EM locks) on a means of egress must fail safely in an emergency.

Fire alarm integration is mandatory for electronic locks on evacuation routes.

The Fail-Safe Requirement: All electronically locked doors on evacuation routes must unlock automatically when the fire alarm activates. This must happen without manual intervention. Integration between the access control and fire alarm panels is mandatory and non-negotiable.

Ask your integrator: "Can you provide documentation showing that our access control system is integrated with the fire alarm panel, and specifically which doors are included?"

Licencing

4. Using a Licenced Integrator

Under the Private Security Industry Act (PSIA), only contractors licenced by the Singapore Police Force (SPF) can install or maintain security systems. The obligation is on the building owner/MCST to ensure they engage a licenced contractor.

Verifying a licence is simple. Securevision's licence is L/PS/000267/2023P. Always verify your contractor's status via the SPF licensing division before signing or renewing a maintenance contract.

Putting It Together

5. A Compliance Checklist for Committees

Reviewing these five questions gives your committee a clear compliance roadmap.
  1. Does our CCTV system have role-based access, a retention policy, and an audit log?
  2. Is our access control system integrated with the fire alarm panel (with documentation)?
  3. Is significant security expenditure being correctly approved at general meetings?
  4. Are detailed security maintenance records being kept and available for audit?
  5. Is our security systems contractor SPF-licenced, and have we verified it?
