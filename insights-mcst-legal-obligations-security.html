What Are the MCST's Legal Obligations for Security Systems?

The MCST's legal responsibilities around security are real, specific, and not well understood. This guide sets out what the law requires, where discretion lies, and how committees can protect themselves against liability.

Wee Meng Ler Founder & CEO · April 2, 2026 · 7 min read
Note: This article provides general information based on the BMSMA and understanding at the time of publication. It is not legal advice.

1. The legal framework — where security obligations come from

The MCST in Singapore is a statutory body created under the Building Maintenance and Strata Management Act (BMSMA). Its core obligation is to maintain and manage the common property for the benefit of all subsidiary proprietors. Security systems — CCTV, access control, intercoms — are common property. Their maintenance is a direct responsibility.

The BMSMA does not prescribe specific equipment standards, but it does require that common property is properly maintained. This means systems must be functional, fit for purpose, and not allowed to degrade to the point where they fail to serve their intended security purpose.

2. The duty to maintain — what it means in practice

Section 29 of the BMSMA places a duty on the MCST to properly maintain common property. For security: Functional systems must remain functional. A known offline camera creates a documented gap. A well-maintained ten-year-old system creates less liability than a new system that the MCST knew had faults and did not address. Systems MUST be maintained by qualified, Police-Licensed contractors.

KEY POINT

The MCST's legal exposure is primarily about whether it has maintained what it has. Documentation of reports and prompt response to faults is critical for defensive risk management.

3. The Personal Data Protection Act — CCTV and resident data

CCTV in common areas is subject to the Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA). Key obligations: Purpose limitation (use footage only for security), Retention limitation (typically 14-30 days), and Data Breach Notification (if the system is compromised). Footage captures residents and visitors — it is personal data that must be managed with care.

4. Procurement obligations — how the MCST must spend

The BMSMA and development by-laws set out a procurement framework. Maintenance within approved budgets is manageable by the MA. Capital expenditure above thresholds requires an ordinary or special resolution at a general meeting. Significant projects usually require at least three competitive quotations to show financial discipline and transparent governance.

Residents exercising their democratic governance rights at an AGM, voting on a resolution for a major security system overhaul.

5. When security failures create liability

Liability is not automatic. What creates it is Negligence: failing to meet a reasonable standard of care. This includes leaving known faults un-repaired, ignoring recommendations from security consultants, or appointing unlicensed contractors. Conversely, regular maintenance and documented decision-making protect the MCST.

6. The committee member's individual position

Members acting in good faith are protected from individual liability; the MCST as a body bears it. However, participating in clear breaches (e.g., awarding major contracts without resident approval) may create exposure. The advice is to follow the required approval process, document decisions, and act on professional technical advice.
