My System Is X Years Old — Should I Upgrade or Repair?

A practical engineering framework for Singapore property owners facing that critical decision on their ageing CCTV, access control or alarm system.

Ler Wee Meng Founder & CEO · Securevision · 8 min read
  • Home
  • Insights
  • My System Is X Years Old — Should I Upgrade or Repair?

The Real Cost of "Just Repairing"

KEY POINTS

Repairing an ageing system feels cheaper upfront. But repeated service calls, parts scarcity, and compatibility gaps can cost more over 24 months than a targeted upgrade.

When a technician visits your site and replaces a faulty DVR board or a seized camera motor, the job is done — on paper. What the invoice does not show is the compounding cost of operating infrastructure that was never designed for today's demands. Analogue DVRs from 2012 record at resolutions that modern investigations find largely unusable. Access control panels from the same era often lack any audit log export, which is now a basic compliance expectation for commercial tenants in Singapore.

The conversation should not start with "how much to fix this?" It should start with "what am I actually getting back for that money?"

The 5-Year Rule Explained

Technical inspection of recording infrastructure is the first step in the framework.

In the security systems industry, five years is the threshold at which the engineering calculus begins to shift. It is not a hard rule, but it reflects a consistent pattern across three variables:

  • Firmware support. Most manufacturers — Hikvision, Dahua, Bosch — issue firmware updates for approximately five to seven years post-manufacture. Once a device exits this window, vulnerabilities go unpatched. In Singapore, where MAS and PDPA compliance increasingly touch physical security infrastructure, an unpatched NVR is an organisational liability.
  • Parts availability. After five years, replacement components for proprietary hardware become harder to source locally. Lead times lengthen. Costs rise. For mission-critical systems — a hospital access control panel, a multi-tenant carpark barrier — downtime carries real operational consequences.
  • Performance gap. The resolution, compression, and analytics capabilities of a 2018 camera versus a 2024 camera are not comparable. H.265+ compression alone can halve your storage footprint. The upgrade often pays for itself in reduced NAS costs within eighteen months.

Signs Your System Has Reached End-of-Life

  • Your DVR or NVR has not received a firmware update in over two years
  • Replacement cameras for your existing system are no longer stocked by local distributors
  • Your recorded footage resolution falls below 1080p full frame
  • You have experienced more than two hardware failures in the past twelve months
  • Your access control system cannot generate a timestamped audit log
  • Remote viewing via your existing app has become unreliable or the app itself is no longer supported
KEY POINTS

Three or more of the above signals indicate your system is in the end-of-life phase. Continued repair spending beyond this point rarely recovers its value.

When Repair Still Makes Sense

Repair is the right answer in three specific scenarios. First, when the system is under five years old and the failure is isolated — a single camera with a damaged cable, a reader with a faulty PCB. The core architecture remains sound and supported.

Second, when you are within twelve to eighteen months of a planned full upgrade. In this window, a targeted repair bridges operations without committing capital prematurely.

Third, when the system is proprietary and the client organisation has a contractual or compliance reason to maintain it in its current form — for instance, a security system integrated into a building management platform that is also being replaced on a fixed schedule.

Outside these three scenarios, repair is often the more expensive long-term choice.

The Hybrid Path: Selective Upgrade

A hybrid approach often yields the best balance of performance and budget.

A full rip-and-replace is rarely necessary and rarely the best use of budget. The more intelligent approach is to audit the system layer by layer and determine which components retain engineering value.

In a typical mixed-age Hikvision system, for example, the cabling infrastructure — conduit, trunking, and cable runs — often has fifteen or more years of useful life remaining. Replacing cameras and the NVR while retaining the cable plant and power infrastructure can achieve a 70 to 80 percent performance improvement at 40 to 50 percent of a full replacement cost.

The same logic applies to access control. A panel that still reads Wiegand cards reliably may simply need a controller upgrade and a new reader head to support mobile credentials — extending the system's useful life by another five to eight years without rewiring a single door.

What a System Assessment Covers

A professional system assessment — not a sales visit — should cover the following areas:

  • Hardware audit. Every device is catalogued: make, model, manufacture date, firmware version, and current operational status. Devices are categorised as retain, repair, or replace.
  • Cabling and infrastructure review. Cable type, condition, and routing are assessed. Coaxial runs that can be converted to IP over coax (using HDCVI or similar) are identified.
  • Software and integration review. VMS platforms, access control software, and third-party integrations are assessed for current vendor support status.
  • Compliance gap analysis. The system is reviewed against relevant standards — for instance, the Singapore Civil Defence Force (SCDF) requirements for fire alarm integration, or MOM workplace monitoring guidelines.
  • Upgrade roadmap. The output is a phased plan — not a single invoice — that sequences replacement in order of urgency and business impact.

Securevision Verdict

If your system is over five years old and you have experienced recurring faults, the honest answer is that continued repair spending is eroding your budget without recovering capability. A structured assessment takes half a day and gives you a defensible, phased roadmap. It is the most efficient first step — and at Securevision, we provide it without obligation. The goal is clarity, not a sales pitch.
