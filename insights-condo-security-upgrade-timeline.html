Realistic Timeline and Disruption Plan for a Condo Security Upgrade

The timeline in most security upgrade proposals is written to win the contract, not to reflect reality. Here is what a realistic schedule actually looks like — and how to plan for the disruption that residents will notice.

Ler Wee Meng Founder & CEO · 37+ Years Experience
  • Home
  • Insights
  • Singapore Industry
  • Project Timeline

1. Why timelines get underestimated

Security upgrade timelines in condominiums are consistently underestimated for three reasons: Procurement takes longer (AGM to contract typically takes 4-8 weeks), Equipment lead times are real (2-6 weeks), and Installation in live premises is slower than empty buildings. Residents are present, and noisy works must be scheduled around their peak usage.

A project that an integrator estimates will take three weeks in an empty building will typically take five to seven weeks in a live condominium. The committee and the MA should plan for the longer duration and communicate accordingly.

2. The realistic project phases

A full condominium security upgrade covering CCTV, access control, and intercom typically runs through these phases:

  • Phase 1 — Preparation (2-4 weeks): Finalise drawings, order equipment, prepare communication.
  • Phase 2 — Infrastructure (1-2 weeks): New cable runs and conduit installation (noisiness).
  • Phase 3 — CCTV Installation (1-2 weeks): Camera installation and NVR setup.
  • Phase 4 — Access Control (1-2 weeks): Reader replacement and card migration.
  • Phase 5 — Intercom Installation (1 week): Door station replacement.
  • Phase 6 — Testing & Handover (1-2 weeks): Final commissioning and staff training.
A detailed infographic showing the seven critical phases of a condominium security upgrade, from award to final resident training.

3. The access card migration — the most underestimated task

In a development of 200 units, each with 2-3 residents, you are distributing 400 to 600 new access cards. Each distribution must be documented. If biometric systems are in use, every user needs to enrol in person. This logistics exercise should not be rushed. Allow a minimum of two weeks for card migration alone.

4. Resident communication — what to send, when

Resident communication is a project management requirement. Pre-start notices should be sent two weeks in advance. Phase-specific notices (e.g., Block 3 corridors) should be sent 3-5 days ahead of work. Access card collection notices are mandatory two weeks before deactivation of old systems.

A Securevision staff member placing a detailed notice to residents in a lobby area, ensuring transparency during the upgrade works.

5. Managing the disruption — what the integrator should provide

The contractor's disruption management plan should address restricted working hours (9am to 5pm), dust and waste management, security during system downtime, and clear contractor identification. Residents seeing unidentified workers in corridors generates anxiety — proper identification (branded Securevision uniforms) and site access controls manage this.

We build our project timelines conservatively because we would rather finish ahead of schedule than explain delays. For every project, we produce a written disruption management plan, provide weekly updates, and ensure our on-site team is identifiable and professional. Residents accept disruption when they are informed.
