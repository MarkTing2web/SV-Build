How to Compare Two Security Integrators Fairly — Even If You Know Nothing About Security Systems

The cheapest quote is rarely the best value. Here is a structured framework for evaluating integrators that goes beyond price — so your committee can make a decision it can defend at any AGM.

Ler Wee Meng Founder & CEO · 37+ Years Experience
  • Home
  • Insights
  • Property Management
  • Comparing Security Integrators

When your MCST invites two or three integrators to quote for a security upgrade, you will almost always receive proposals that are impossible to compare directly. One quotes for 24 cameras. Another quotes for 16. One includes a five-year maintenance contract. Another quotes only for installation.

Proposals that cannot be compared directly protect the integrator, not the client. When your committee cannot make a like-for-like comparison, the decision defaults to the number at the bottom of the page — and the cheapest number wins, regardless of what it actually includes.

This article gives you a framework for comparing integrators that goes beyond price. It takes approximately two hours to apply properly. It will save your estate from the most common and most costly mistake in security procurement.

Before You Compare Anything

Step One — Legal Verification

This step takes two minutes and immediately eliminates any integrator who should not be on your shortlist.

Under the Private Security Industry Act, only contractors licensed by the Singapore Police Force are permitted to install, maintain, or repair security systems in Singapore. Ask for every integrator's SPF licence number before you evaluate their proposal.

Engaging an unlicensed contractor exposes your MCST to legal liability that no price saving justifies. Beyond the SPF licence, check for BCA registration and bizSAFE certification. These indicate a company that operates at a professional standard across multiple regulatory frameworks.

Making the Proposals Comparable

Step Two — Build a Scope Table

Do not look at the price until you have extracted the following information into a simple comparison table. Unstated scope is unbudgeted scope — it will appear as an additional cost later.

Five Critical Scope Items to Verify:

  • Camera Specifications: Are they quoting the same resolution and retention days at full speed?
  • Mobile Integration: Is the app included or is it a separate per-user license fee?
  • Cabling: Is the infrastructure new or assumed reusable (a common trap)?
  • Warranty: Does it cover labor or just hardware parts replacement?
  • Handover: Are as-built drawings and staff training included?
Understanding Price Differences

Step Three — When One Quote is Significantly Cheaper

A price difference of 10–15% is normal. A difference of 30% or more almost always means one of four things:

  • Scope is different: The cheaper quote is just a smaller job (e.g., fewer cameras or omitted cabling).
  • Brands are different: Hardware with no local distributor or manufacturer support is cheaper upfront but impossible to service.
  • Installation standard: Cut-price installation skips surge protection and proper containment. Ask to visit a completed project.
  • Post-installation model: A low installation price often masks high margin expectations for reactive maintenance call-outs.
Evaluating Long-Term Fit

Step Four — The Five-Year Test

Security is a relationship, not a transaction. Switching integrators mid-lifecycle is expensive and disruptive.

The right integrator builds a long-term working relationship with the committee.

Three Questions to Ask:

  1. "Can you show me a project you installed 5+ years ago that you still maintain today?" (Retaining clients is a signal of quality).
  2. "Who is my named contact after installation?" (Ensures accountability beyond the sales team).
  3. "What is your process when a component reaches end-of-life?" (Separates transactional sellers from lifecycle managers).
Reference Checks

Step Five — Verification

Ask for contacts at similar residential estates. When you call them, don't ask if they are "happy." Ask specific questions:

  • "When something went wrong, how did they respond?"
  • "Is the system actually working the way they promised during the sales demo?"
  • "Would you use them again for your next upgrade?"
Final Evaluation

Step Six — The Scoring Summary

Score each integrator on a three-point scale (Strong, Adequate, Weak) across: Credentials, Scope Clarity, Brand Quality, References, and Response Time Commitment. The integrator with the most "Strong" ratings is your best choice — even if they aren't the cheapest.
